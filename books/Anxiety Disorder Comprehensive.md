# Part 1: Epidemiology and Genetics

## 1. Epidemiology of Anxiety Disorders
**Authors:** T. Overbeek, E. Vermetten, and E.J.L. Griez (Maastricht University, Maastricht, The Netherlands)

### 1.1 Introduction
Anxiety disorders have a high impact on daily life (illness intrusiveness) and cause a great deal of suffering for the individual patient (Antony et al., 1998). They also have a substantial economic impact, with direct and indirect expenses estimated at $42.3 billion per year in the USA (Greenberg et al., 1999). European countries likely face a similar economic burden (Costa, 1998; Martin, 1998).

Major epidemiological studies such as the Epidemiologic Catchment Area study (ECA), the National Comorbidity Survey (NCS) in the USA, and the Munich Follow-up study in Europe provide crucial prevalence data. The WHO Study on Psychological Problems in General Health Care also offers insight into mental disorders in primary care across 14 countries. 

**Challenges in Epidemiology:**
* Different diagnostic instruments and case definitions.
* Varied sampling procedures and severity ratings.
* Time frames for diagnoses vary (e.g., lifetime, six-month, current).

**Treatment Gap:**
Knowledge of prevalence does not equate to adequate treatment. There are two main filters preventing proper care:
1. Only about 50% of cases are recognized and correctly diagnosed by a general practitioner.
2. Only half of the recognized cases receive the most adequate treatment.
Consequently, only roughly 25% of disordered subjects receive adequate treatment. Additionally, some disorders like specific phobias (prevalence 11%) are managed via avoidance and require less clinical intervention compared to more severe conditions like Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD, prevalence 2-3%).

**General Prevalence and Risk Factors:**
* Anxiety disorders are the most common psychiatric disorders (25%), followed by major depression (17%) (Kessler et al., 1994).
* Lifetime prevalence for all anxiety disorders combined (NCS): 19.2% for men, 30.5% for women.
* Strong correlation between lower socio-economic status and anxiety disorders.
* High comorbidity complicates interpretation. Anxiety disorders often co-exist with depressive disorders (30% concurrent to 60% lifetime). Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) and Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD) have comorbidity rates of up to 90% and 80%, respectively. Medical comorbidities (cardiac, pulmonary, gastrointestinal) are also common.

### 1.2 Panic Disorder and Agoraphobia
According to DSM-IV, panic attacks are sudden spells of unidentified feelings with at least four out of 13 symptoms (e.g., palpitations, sweating, shortness of breath, trembling, derealisation, fear of dying). Panic Disorder (PD) requires that at least one attack has been unexpected, followed by a month of fearful expectation or concern. Agoraphobia often follows or accompanies PD and is defined as fear of being in places where escape might be difficult.

**Prevalence:**
* Isolated panic attacks: 7-9% lifetime prevalence.
* ECA study: 15% lifetime panic attack prevalence, 3% past month. 1% meet criteria for PD in the past month.
* Lifetime prevalence for PD: 1.5% to 2.5%. Twelve-month prevalence: ~1%.
* In clinical practice, finding an agoraphobic without a history of panic is infrequent. However, Wittchen et al. (1998) found that the majority of confirmed agoraphobics did not have a prior history of panic. NCS data indicates a 6.7% lifetime prevalence for agoraphobia as a separate diagnosis.

**Demographics and Risk Factors:**
* Higher rates for women (panic attacks are twice as common in women).
* Age at onset: mid-twenties. Hazard rates for women: 25-35 years; for men: 30-45 years.
* Marital status: Highest lifetime prevalence in widowed, separated, or divorced subjects.
* Educational level: Individuals with less than 12 years of education may have a tenfold higher risk (Eaton et al., 1994).
* Other risk factors: Smoking, pulmonary disease in childhood (bronchitis, asthma), and chronic obstructive pulmonary disorders.

**Natural Course:**
* PD and agoraphobia are chronic conditions with a fluctuating course.
* Long-term studies show low remission rates (e.g., only 12% in full remission after five years). Highly disabling, affecting social and professional functioning.

**Comorbidity:**
* PD and agoraphobia commonly co-occur (29.5% to 58.2%).
* High comorbidity with Social Phobia (20-75%), GAD (20%), OCD (14%), and PTSD (6%).
* Major depression is the most frequent comorbid diagnosis (30-60% concurrent, 55.6% to 60% lifetime). Onset order varies.
* Substance abuse (alcohol, drugs) is present in 36% of cases, often interpreted as self-medication.

### 1.3 Specific Phobias
Defined as a circumscribed, persistent, and unreasonable fear of a particular object or situation. Exposure causes an acute anxiety reaction. The individual recognises the fear is unrealistic but avoids the stimulus. DSM-IV subtypes include animal, situational, blood-injury, and nature-environment phobias.

**Prevalence:**
* NCS rates: 5.5% (one-month) and 11.3% (lifetime).
* Blood-injury phobia: 3.5% lifetime prevalence; mean onset age is 5.5 years.

**Demographics and Risk Factors:**
* Female to male ratio: 2.3 : 1. Lifetime prevalence: 15.7% (females) vs 6.7% (males).
* Fear of animals is most frequent in women; fear of heights in men.

**Natural Course:**
* Chronic illness of moderate severity. Behavioural treatments are effective but infrequently utilised.

**Comorbidity:**
* 83.4% of persons with simple phobia reported at least one lifetime comorbid disorder (frequently other anxiety disorders or depression).

### 1.4 Social Phobias
Characterised by a marked fear of performance, excessive fear of scrutiny, and fear of acting embarrassingly. Divided into generalised (anxious in most interactional situations) and non-generalised (fear of specific situations like public speaking). Generalised type has a stronger genetic factor and less chance of spontaneous recovery. Differential diagnoses include avoidant personality disorder.

**Prevalence:**
* Third most common psychiatric disorder (13.3% lifetime prevalence), exceeded only by major depression and alcohol dependence.
* 4.5% to 4.9% one-month prevalence. Often underdiagnosed by general practitioners.

**Demographics and Risk Factors:**
* Slight preponderance in women (1.5 times as many as men; 15.5% vs 11.1%). However, men are more likely to seek treatment.
* Feared items differ by gender (e.g., eating/writing in public for men; public speaking/using public restrooms for women).
* More frequent in younger ages (18-29), less educated, single, and lower socio-economic classes. Associated with higher unemployment and absenteeism.

**Natural Course:**
* Onset typically in puberty, often preceded by early shyness.
* Chronic and unremitting, heavily influencing career and relationship development.

**Comorbidity:**
* Around 80% co-exist with other disorders. Lifetime depression is about 70%.
* Other anxiety comorbidities: PD (10.9% to 49%), GAD (13.3% to 32%), specific phobia (37.6% to 60.8%).
* Alcoholism prevalence is high (14-40% among social phobics), often predating alcohol problems as an attempt to self-medicate.

### 1.5 Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
Characterised by recurrent obsessions (persistent thoughts/impulses) or compulsions (repetitive behaviors) that are excessively time-consuming (>1 hour/day) and cause marked distress. Core features include abnormal risk assessment, pathologic doubt, and incompleteness.

**Prevalence:**
* Previously thought rare, now recognized as a "hidden epidemic."
* Six-month prevalence: ~1%. Lifetime prevalence: 2-3%.
* Cross-national annual rates range from 1.1/100 (Korea/New Zealand) to 1.8/100 (Puerto Rico), with Taiwan exceptionally low (0.4/100).

**Demographics and Risk Factors:**
* Age of onset: mid-to-late twenties. 
* Gender ratio: Often reported as equal (1:1), though some studies show slight female preponderance.

**Natural Course:**
* Chronic course. Spontaneous remission is rare.
* In a 40-year follow-up, 20% completely recovered, 28% recovered with subclinical symptoms, 35% improved but remained symptomatic.

**Comorbidity:**
* Depression is the most frequent complication (one-third concurrent, up to two-thirds lifetime in clinical populations). OCD usually precedes depression.
* Personality disorders frequently diagnosed but may remit with effective OCD treatment.
* High comorbidity with Panic Disorder (54%), Social Phobia (42%), Eating Disorders (17%), and Tourette's (5%).

### 1.6 Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
Characterised by chronic, excessive worry and physical symptoms like tension and restlessness. Controversial status as an independent entity due to exceptionally high comorbidity.

**Prevalence:**
* NCS rates: 1.6% current, 3.1% 12-month, 5.1% lifetime. 
* Most frequently diagnosed anxiety disorder in primary care, but least common in mental health centers. Often underdiagnosed.

**Demographics and Risk Factors:**
* Twice as common in women. Highest prevalence in 25-35 age group.
* Correlates: Being separated/widowed/divorced, unemployment, or being a homemaker.

**Natural Course:**
* Chronic course, demonstrating greater diagnostic stability and less improvement over time compared to Panic Disorder.

**Comorbidity:**
* Lifetime comorbidity is extremely high (90.4%).
* Major depression (62.4%), dysthymia (39.5%). Other anxiety disorders: Specific phobia (35.1%), Social phobia (34.4%), Agoraphobia (25.7%), Panic disorder (23.5%).
* Substance abuse: Alcohol (37.6%), drugs (27.6%).
* Frequently associated with unexplained somatic complaints (chest pain, irritable bowel, chronic fatigue).

### 1.7 Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
Requires exposure to a traumatic event, re-experiencing, avoidance/numbing, and increased arousal lasting at least one month. Trauma is commonplace (childhood abuse, accidents, assault, combat, disasters).

**Prevalence:**
* NCS lifetime prevalence: 7.8%.
* High incidence following specific traumas (e.g., 23% after traffic accident/fire, 34.3% after Oklahoma City bombing, ~30-37% for childhood abuse victims).
* Often underdiagnosed if not the presenting complaint.

**Demographics and Risk Factors:**
* Higher among women and those widowed/separated/divorced.
* Common traumas: Combat exposure/witnessing violence for men; rape/sexual molestation for women.
* Risk factors: Characteristics of the stressor (repeated trauma), female gender, younger age, psychiatric history, family/cultural factors.

**Natural Course:**
* Over one-third fail to recover even after many years.
* Median remission time: 36 months with professional help, 64 months without.
* Symptoms may plateau and become chronic. Substance abuse often parallels symptom increase.

**Comorbidity:**
* Highly comorbid: 88.3% of men and 79% of women with lifetime PTSD have at least one other disorder.
* Affective disorders (50% major depression), specific phobia (30%), social phobia (28%), alcohol use (52% in men, 28% in women), somatisation.

### 1.8 Conclusion
Despite expanding epidemiological data, caution must be exercised in interpreting findings due to methodological variations, the frequent use of lay interviewers in surveys, recall biases, and differing definitions of clinical significance.

## 2. Genetics of Anxiety Disorders: Part I
**Authors:** M.C. Cavallini and L. Bellodi (Fondazione Centro San Raffaele del Monte Tabor, Milan, Italy)

### 2.1 Introduction
Anxiety disorders are a heterogeneous group of psychiatric disorders with no clear knowledge of their aetiology and pathogenesis. Several familial, biological, and genetic risk factors have been invoked for obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and panic disorder (PD). The role of biological factors is supported by pharmacological treatment outcomes and specific anomalies found in neuro-imaging (PET, NMR) for some OCD and PD patients. Furthermore, the presence of secondary cases in families of probands affected with anxiety disorders suggests a familial component and a genetically transmissible basis.

### 2.2 Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
The evidence of a genetic component in OCD is derived mainly from twin and familial studies. The relationship between Tourette's Syndrome (TS) and OCD has heavily influenced genetic research, as patients with TS frequently have an OCD co-diagnosis, and their relatives show significantly increased morbidity risk for OCD.

**Twin and Familial Studies:**
* Twin studies indicate a genetic basis, though monozygotic (MZ) concordance is lower than 100%, indicating non-genetic factors are also present.
    * Carey and Gottesman (1981): 87% of MZ co-twins had obsessive symptoms versus 47% of dizygotic (DZ) co-twins.
* Familial studies show increased morbidity risks, although variable depending on sampling (e.g., early onset vs. adult onset).
    * Early onset (< 14 years) probands have higher rates of illness among relatives (8.8%) compared to later onset probands (3.4%).
    * Pauls et al. (1995) found an 18.2% morbidity risk when including sub-threshold secondary OCD cases, indicating familial and heterogeneous conditions.
* Segregation studies evaluating OCD and TS together suggest a relatively simple genetic model might explain the inheritance pattern, such as a Mendelian Dominant model with incomplete penetrance.

**Molecular Genetics:**
* **Serotonergic Pathways:** Despite the efficacy of Serotonin Re-uptake Inhibitors (SRI), case control studies on 5HT2c and 5HT2a receptors, as well as the serotonin transporter gene, have largely excluded a major effect of these elements in OCD development for the studied populations.
* **Dopaminergic Pathways:** No positive findings are available for association studies with DRD2 and DRD3, though a variant of the D4 receptor is increased in OCD patients with tics.
* **Other Findings:** A sexually dimorphic effect exists for the Catechol-O-Methyl-Transferase (COMT) gene and Monoamine Oxidase-A (MAO-A) gene, with specific alleles associated with male OCD patients (and co-morbid Major Depressive Disorder).

### 2.3 Panic Disorder (PD)
Similar to OCD, familial and twin studies support the existence of heredity in PD, but the definition of genetic components is not yet well established.

**Twin and Familial Studies:**
* Familial risks for first-degree relatives range from 3.4% to 17%.
* Age of onset differentiates familial subtypes: relatives of probands with PD onset at or before 20 years of age show a higher risk (22%) than those with later onset (8%).
* Twin studies confirm higher concordance in MZ twin pairs (e.g., 24% for MZ vs. 11% for DZ in a large study by Kendler et al., 1993, with heritability estimates of 35% to 46%).
* Segregation studies suggest a Mendelian mode of transmission, though results vary between Dominant models and Single Major Locus (SML) with a polygenic background.

**Molecular Genetics:**
* Linkage studies have mostly been disappointing or contrasting.
* **Cholecystokinin (CCK):** Evidence suggests PD might be associated with abnormal CCK function. Polymorphisms in both CCK-A and CCK-B receptor genes have shown significant association or transmission disequilibrium with PD.
* **Adenosine and MAO-A:** A positive association has been found with an A2a adenosine receptor variant, and an excess of high activity MAO-A promoter alleles has been noted in female patients with PD.
* **Biological Markers (CO2 Challenge):** The 35% CO2 challenge test helps define biological determinants. A positive response (panic attack) to CO2 inhalation is highly familial (14.4% risk vs. 3.9% in families of non-responders). MZ twin concordance for this response is 55.6% vs. 12.5% for DZ twins, strongly suggesting a genetic basis (likely a dominant Mendelian model) for the "respiratory" subtype of PD.

### 2.4 Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
While acute traumatic stress leads to PTSD, familial factors—especially familial anxiety or vulnerability to major depression—serve as determinants of risk. 
* A genetic component may influence the probability of exposure to specific traumatic environments (heritability estimates of 35% to 47% for combat exposure).
* An association study showed linkage disequilibrium with an allelic variant of the DRD2 receptor gene, conferring an increased risk (or relative resistance) to PTSD.

### 2.5 Conclusion
Findings in the genetics of anxiety disorders are currently limited by several factors:
1. Diagnostic and phenotypic boundaries are not completely defined, resulting in heterogeneous samples.
2. Over-simplified models (e.g., assuming a Single Major Locus) may miss genes with small effects or complex polygenic backgrounds.
3. Gene-environment interactions are complex and modify gene expression.
4. Statistical techniques (like linkage analysis) have limitations in identifying minor effect genes.

## 3. Genetics of Anxiety Disorders: Part II
**Authors:** B. de Brettes and J.P. Lépine (Hôpital Fernand Widal, Paris, France)

### 3.1 Introduction
Due to the extremely fast development of molecular genetic methods in the past 10 years, more studies have been carried out on genetic factors of various psychiatric disorders, including anxiety disorders. Familial aggregation of anxiety disorders has been repeatedly reported, but this phenomenon may be explained by various aetiologic factors, namely familial environment and genes. Early theories suggested an inherited tendency to anxiety, while recent family, twin, linkage, and association studies have produced conflicting but sometimes positive results. Complex disorders like anxiety pose challenges due to the interaction of environmental effects with a set of genes, where each accounts only for a small part in the liability of the disorder.

### 3.2 Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
Genetics studies of GAD were heavily influenced by the progressive modifications of diagnostic systems (from DSM-III to DSM-IV). The increasingly restrictive criteria for GAD sought more reliability, which results in a more independent familial transmission of GAD from other anxiety or depressive disorders.

**Familial Aggregation Studies:**
* **Cloninger et al. (1981):** Found no significant excess of anxiety disorders among the first-degree relatives of anxious subjects with a GAD-like syndrome compared with control probands.
* **Noyes et al. (1987):** Using DSM-III criteria, rates of GAD appeared significantly higher among 123 relatives of "pure GAD" (without panic disorder or attacks) probands compared to relatives of non-psychiatrically ill subjects (19.5% vs. 3.5%). The familial risk for GAD appears specific for the disorder, as the frequency was not higher among relatives of panic or agoraphobic probands.
* **Reich (1995):** Confirmed family predisposition in a male population using family history methods. The prevalence of GAD among relatives of GAD probands (12.7%) was significantly higher compared with relatives of subjects with major depressive disorder (6.8%) or control subjects (1.9%).

**Twin Studies:**
Three studies used the conservative, purely descriptive k statistical measure of twin concordance and found no significant differences between Monozygotic (MZ) and Dizygotic (DZ) twins:
* **Torgersen (1983):** MZ 0%, DZ 5%
* **Andrews et al. (1990):** MZ 20.6%, DZ 13.6%
* **Skre et al. (1993):** MZ 60%, DZ 14% (All cases had a lifetime history of mood disorder).

Using tetrachoric correlation coefficients, **Kendler et al. (1992a)** estimated the liability of heredity of GAD to be moderate, ranging from 19% to 30%. The remainder of the variance seems to result from individual-specific environmental experiences rather than familial ones. **Roy et al. (1995)** also found heritability remains moderate (49.0% with broad definitions to 14.3% with narrower definitions). There remain doubts about the heredity of "pure" GAD; some authors suggest GAD is hereditary only when there is a comorbid history of Major Depressive Disorder (MDD).

### 3.3 Phobias
All phobias show an irrational and fearful avoidance of objects or situations but differ in age at onset, comorbidity, and phobic stimulus. A central question is whether each phobia has a specific familial aggregation.

**Social Phobia (SP):**
* **Familial Transmission:** Fyer et al. (1995) found DSM-III-R Social Phobia is associated with a significant but moderate familial risk (relative risk: 2.5). Rates of other anxiety disorders did not differ significantly.
* **Generalised vs. Non-Generalised:** Relatives of patients with generalised SP have a significantly greater rate of SP (16%) than relatives of non-generalised SP (6%) or controls (6%).
* **Twin Studies:** Kendler et al. (1992c) showed a less than two times higher probandwise concordance for SP in MZ compared with DZ twins, estimating the genetic heritability of liability at 30%.

**Agoraphobia:**
* **Familial Aggregation:** Moran and Andrews (1985) and Noyes et al. (1986) confirmed familial aggregation of agoraphobia. Risk is significantly increased among relatives of agoraphobics but not relatives of PD patients or non-anxious subjects.
* **Twin Studies:** Kendler et al. (1992c) found the heredity of liability for agoraphobia was 39% in a female twin population, with the rest of the variance due to individual-specific environmental effects.

**Specific Phobias:**
* **Situational Phobias:** A moderate but statistically significant specific familial aggregation was found (relative risk: 3.9). However, Kendler et al. (1992c) failed to show a clear genetic effect, suggesting disease liability was solely the result of individual specific and familial environmental influences.
* **Animal Phobias:** Disease liability may result from additive genetic effects (32% of the variance) and individual specific environmental effects (68%).

### 3.4 Conclusion
There appears to be a general consensus that the different categories of anxiety disorders are familial and probably have a moderate genetic component. Identifying susceptibility genes involved in anxiety disorders could inspire new, more effective therapies and help understand the heterogeneity among these disorders. However, a serious lack of basic knowledge remains, especially regarding to what extent genes contribute to phobias and GAD. 

Future research must focus on refining phenotypes. Additionally, an alternative research field investigating anxiety proneness with a dimensional approach (based on personality traits like neuroticism, shyness, or harm avoidance) may be promising, as these traits have been shown to have a substantial genetic variance.

# Part 2: Nosology and Treatment of Anxiety Disorders


## 4. Panic Disorder: Clinical Course, Morbidity and Comorbidity
**Authors:** C. Faravelli and A. Paionni (Florence University Medical School, Florence, Italy)

### 4.1 Introduction
With the abolition of the term "neurosis" and the consequent reclassification of anxiety states, recurrent crises of acute anxiety (panic attacks), whether associated with agoraphobia or not, have acquired nosological autonomy (DSM-III, DSM-III-R, DSM-IV). The recognition of the wide prevalence of panic disorder (PD), its consequences, its physiological concomitants, and the discovery that panic can be induced by chemical cues have stimulated intense interest. PD is presently seen as the most typical, and probably the core of anxiety disorders.

### 4.2 The Panic Attack
A panic attack is defined as a discrete period of intense fear or discomfort accompanied by somatic and psychic symptoms. The attack has a sudden onset and rapidly builds to a peak (usually in 10 minutes or less). It is accompanied by a sense of imminent danger or impending doom and an urge to escape.

**Somatic and Systemic Symptoms:**
* Cardiovascular (palpitations, pounding heart, accelerated heart rate).
* Respiratory (dyspnea, chest pain, sensations of shortness of breath or smothering).
* Neurological-like (dizziness, trembling, paresthesias).
* Sweating, nausea, abdominal distress, chills or hot flushes.
* Often, somatic symptoms mask anxiety, leading patients to seek non-psychiatric medical help.

**Psychic Symptoms:**
* Feelings of dizziness, unsteadiness, or fainting.
* Derealisation or depersonalisation.
* Fear of losing control, going crazy, or dying. 

**Context and Differential Diagnosis:**
Symptoms of panic can be present in medical conditions (e.g., hyperthyroidism, pulmonary embolism, hypoglycemia, arrhythmias). Therefore, DSM-IV criteria explicitly require the exclusion of organic causes. Panic attacks can also occur in other psychiatric conditions (phobias, major depression, OCD, etc.) and in healthy people.

**Types of Panic Attacks:**
1. **Unexpected panic attacks:** Unpredictable, occurring "out of the blue." Patients more often report dizziness, paresthesia, and fear of going crazy/losing control.
2. **Situationally bound panic attacks:** Invariably occur immediately on exposure to a situational cue.
3. **Situationally predisposed panic attacks:** More likely to occur on exposure to a cue, but not invariably.

**Severity:**
DSM-IV distinguishes "full-blown" attacks (at least four of 13 symptoms) from "limited-symptom" attacks (fewer than four symptoms). Limited-symptom attacks are common in PD and may persist even when major attacks decrease.

**Nocturnal Panic Attacks:**
Characterised by sudden awakening, terror, and hyperarousal. Nearly 40% of PD patients have panic attacks during sleep, which are non-REM sleep events.

### 4.3 Panic Disorder
PD is a pathological condition characterised by recurrent panic attacks, combined with long-lasting symptoms and attitudes present between attacks (e.g., persistent concern about having another attack, worry about implications, or significant behavioural changes). Associated features include agoraphobia, hypochondriasis, anticipatory anxiety, and demoralisation.

**Recurrent Panic Attacks:**
Patients typically experience a mix of unexpected, situationally predisposed, and situationally bound attacks. Over time, full-blown attacks may decrease while limited-symptom attacks persist.

**Anticipatory Anxiety:**
Following the first attack, patients develop a fear of future attacks, raising the level of diffuse, non-panic anxiety. This hyperarousal can lower the threshold for new attacks and lead to avoidance behaviour.

**Agoraphobia:**
Defined as anxiety about being in places/situations where escape might be difficult or help unavailable in the event of a panic attack. This leads to pervasive avoidance (e.g., being alone outside, being in crowds, travelling). Avoidance ranges from mild uneasiness to severe, invalidating distress where the patient cannot leave home.
* In psychiatric samples, 75% of PD patients present some degree of agoraphobia (30-50% in epidemiological surveys).
* The relationship between panic and agoraphobia remains debated: Does panic cause agoraphobia (the North American view), or does a pre-existing phobic attitude predispose one to panic?

**Hypochondriasis (Interoceptive Conditioning):**
Patients develop exaggerated sensitivity to bodily sensations. Somatic symptoms become conditioned stimuli; normal physical sensations (e.g., from exercise or caffeine) can trigger anxiety or full panic attacks. 

**Demoralisation:**
Around 30% of patients develop feelings of sadness, guilt, or anhedonia due to the social and personal impairment caused by the disorder.

### 4.4 Epidemiology
* **Prevalence:** Annual rates range from 1.7/100 (West Germany) to 0.2/100 (Taiwan). Lifetime prevalence of PD with agoraphobia is around 1.5%.
* **Socio-economic factors:** Lower educational attainment correlates heavily with PD risk (individuals with less than 12 years of education are over 10 times more likely to have PD).
* **Gender differences:** Women are over twice as likely to suffer from PD and represent over three-quarters of PD patients with extensive agoraphobic avoidance. Men have a longer duration of illness but less frequent phobic/depressive complications.

### 4.5 Onset and Antecedents
* **Age of Onset:** Typically in the early to mid-twenties, showing a bimodal distribution (15-24 years and a later mode at 45-54 years). Early onset (<20 years) indicates a higher familial/genetic risk.
* **Context of First Attack:** Over 90% of agoraphobic patients experience their first attack in a public, phobogenic situation, which may heavily influence the subsequent development of agoraphobia.
* **Stressful Life Events:** A high concentration of life stress (e.g., major loss or separation) often occurs in the months preceding the first attack. 
* **Early Life Events:** Childhood trauma (abuse, separation) increases the risk for anxiety disorders generally. Childhood separation anxiety, in particular, is strongly linked to the later development of adult PD and agoraphobia.
* **Maternal Over-protection:** PD patients frequently report having over-protective, less caring parents, though it is difficult to isolate whether this is a cause of the disorder or a reaction to the child's early anxious traits.
* **Personality Traits:** PD is associated with dependent, avoidant, and borderline personality traits, and high levels of neuroticism.

### 4.6 Clinical Course and Morbidity
* **Course:** Generally chronic with waxing and waning. After a 5-year follow-up, only 10-12% of patients fully recover.
* **Morbidity:** PD is associated with poor physical/emotional health, financial dependence, and a substantial decrease in quality of life.
* **Suicidality:** There is a remarkably high rate of suicidal ideation and suicide attempts among PD patients (up to 42% in some outpatient samples). This risk is often mediated by comorbid depression and substance abuse.

### 4.7 Comorbidity
PD rarely exists independently; it is highly comorbid with other psychiatric and medical conditions.
* **Other Anxiety Disorders:** Lifetime comorbidity with at least one other anxiety disorder is 37%. GAD and social phobia are the most frequent comorbid diagnoses.
* **Depression:** Highly comorbid. While early hypotheses suggested depression is secondary to the demoralisation of agoraphobia, empirical evidence shows that patients with and without histories of depression have had PD for similar lengths of time.
* **Alcohol and Substance Abuse:** Often starts as "self-medication" for anticipatory anxiety but becomes a severe complication. The "kindling" process of repeated alcohol withdrawal may also lower the threshold for panic in susceptible individuals.
* **Medical Comorbidities:** Panic is highly associated with migraine (especially migraine with aura), irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), vestibular dysfunction (especially in agoraphobic patients), and histories of traumatic suffocation.

## 5. Panic Disorder: Pathogenesis and Treatment
**Authors:** C. Faravelli, V. Ricca, and E. Truglia (Florence University Medical School, Florence, Italy)

### 5.1 Introduction
The origin of panic disorder (PD) remains an open question. Research into the pathogenesis of PD has focused on biological findings (provocative agents and neurotransmitter systems), neuroanatomical models, and psychological models (psychodynamic, behavioural, and cognitive). Treatment approaches integrate both pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy.

### 5.2 Pathogenesis

#### Biological Findings
**Provocative Agents:**
* **Sodium Lactate & $CO_2$:** Intravenous infusion of sodium lactate (0.5-1 M) and inhalation of $CO_2$ (5% for 20 minutes or a single 35% breath) reliably induce panic attacks in PD patients. This supports Klein's "false suffocation alarm" theory, which posits that PD patients have a low threshold in their physiological "asphisiostat."
* **Yohimbine:** An alpha-2 adrenergic antagonist that increases noradrenergic firing, inducing panic and suggesting noradrenergic involvement.
* **Caffeine:** High doses can be anxiogenic or panicogenic, and PD patients show heightened sensitivity compared to controls.
* **Serotonergic Agents:** m-chlorophenylpiperazine (mCPP) and fenfluramine provoke panic attacks, suggesting a role for 5-HT.
* **CCK-4:** Cholecystokinin-tetrapeptide is panicogenic in humans.

**Neurotransmitter Systems:**
* **Noradrenergic:** Hyperactivity of the locus coeruleus (LC) is hypothesized, potentially due to a dysfunction of noradrenergic receptors.
* **Serotonergic (5-HT):** The relationship is complex, with two opposing hypotheses: a 5-HT excess (increased release or postsynaptic supersensitivity) versus a 5-HT deficit (reduced restraint on panic behavior).
* **GABA-ergic:** A shift in the benzodiazepine receptor set point in the inverse agonist direction or a deficiency of an anxiolytic ligand is proposed.
* **Other Systems:** Dopaminergic and opioid (e.g., naltrexone-induced panic) dysfunctions, cholinergic abnormalities, and neuromodulators (CCK and CRH) are also implicated.

#### Neuroanatomical & Neuroimaging Findings
* **Neuroanatomy:** Gray (1988) proposed the septo-hippocampal system as the mediator of anxiety. Gorman et al. (1989) formulated a model involving three areas: the brainstem (mediating somatic symptoms/panic), the limbic lobe (anticipatory anxiety), and the prefrontal cortex (phobic avoidance).
* **Neuroimaging:** MRI studies have shown anatomical abnormalities in the mesiotemporal region. PET/SPECT studies reveal cerebral hypervasoconstriction during hyperventilation and panic attacks, with abnormal functioning noted in the anterior insular cortex and anterior temporal regions.

#### Psychological Models
* **Psychodynamic:** Freud viewed anxiety initially as a failure of defence mechanisms, and later as a trigger for them. Modern theories emphasize parenting styles and early experiences as creating vulnerability.
* **Behavioural:** Early models focused on classical conditioning, where physiological sensations (e.g., palpitations) are paired with an unconditioned stimulus (e.g., hyperventilation) to trigger panic.
* **Cognitive:** * *Clark's Theory:* PD patients catastrophically misinterpret normal bodily sensations (e.g., a slight breathlessness as impending death), creating a vicious cycle of anxiety.
    * *Barlow's False Alarm Theory:* Identifies three alarms: true, false (spontaneous panic), and learned (conditioned panic). It involves a biological diathesis combined with psychological vulnerability, leading to anxious apprehension and interoceptive conditioning.
    * *Anxiety Sensitivity:* A belief that anxiety symptoms have deleterious physical or psychological consequences, regardless of immediate physical discomfort. It is normally distributed but significantly higher in PD patients.

#### Areas of Controversy
* **The American View (Biological):** Panic is a primary, biological event. Agoraphobia is strictly secondary to panic. Supported by chemical provocation, EEG findings (non-REM sleep panics), and specific pharmacological responses.
* **The Psychological View:** Panic is a severe form of general anxiety. Agoraphobia can precede panic (supported by epidemiological surveys and prodromal symptom reports) and is based on a pre-existing phobic or dependent personality.
* **The Evolutionary Perspective:** Panic symptoms are expressions of an ortho-sympathetic stimulation, adaptive in animals for "fight or flight," but dysregulated in PD patients.

### 5.3 Pharmacological Treatment
Antidepressants and high-potency benzodiazepines (BDZs) are the mainstays of pharmacological treatment. Efficacy is generally measured by the reduction or blockade of panic attacks.

**Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs):**
* Imipramine and clomipramine are the most studied.
* Standard doses range from 150 to 250 mg/day (up to 300 mg/day).
* Latency to response is typically 4 to 8 weeks.
* Side effects (anticholinergic, cardiovascular, weight gain) limit compliance; up to 35% of patients drop out.

**Selective Serotonin Re-uptake Inhibitors (SSRIs):**
* Paroxetine, fluvoxamine, sertraline, and citalopram have shown efficacy in double-blind, placebo-controlled trials.
* SSRIs are effective against panic attacks, anticipatory anxiety, and depressive symptoms, with fewer side effects than TCAs, leading to better compliance. They are generally considered the first-line pharmacological treatment.

**Benzodiazepines (BDZs):**
* Alprazolam is highly effective (doses up to 6-10 mg/day, mean ~3 mg/day), with a rapid onset of action (days vs. weeks for TCAs).
* Clonazepam is also effective (mean dose 2 mg/day) and has a longer half-life, which may reduce inter-dose rebound anxiety.
* Drawbacks include sedation, memory impairment, and significant difficulties with dependence and withdrawal (rebound panic upon discontinuation).

**Other Drugs & Caveats:**
* Various other agents (GABA-B agonists, calcium-channel blockers) show limited efficacy in double-blind trials.
* Clinical trials do not always reflect real-world complexities (e.g., long-term use, comorbidities, subjective intolerance).

### 5.4 Psychological Treatment
Psychological approaches, particularly Cognitive-Behavioural Therapy (CBT), have proven highly effective for PD and agoraphobia.
* **Efficacy:** CBT is more effective than no treatment or psychosocial placebos, and matches or exceeds some pharmacological interventions.
* **Mechanism:** Therapies based on Clark's or Barlow's models focus on educating the patient, identifying "automatic thoughts," and using strategies like probability revaluation and decatastrophising to correct cognitive errors regarding bodily sensations.
* **Combined Approach:** While drugs offer faster relief, their effects often wane after discontinuation. Psychological treatments provide enduring coping skills. A combined approach is the preferred choice in clinical practice.

## 6. Specific Phobias
**Authors:** H. Merckelbach and P. Muris (Maastricht University, Maastricht, The Netherlands)

### 6.1 Symptomatology
DSM-IV defines specific phobia as an intense, excessive, and unreasonable fear directed at a limited set of stimuli, resulting in avoidance behaviour that interferes with daily life. 
* **Non-Random Distribution:** Fears are not randomly distributed. Specific fears (e.g., spiders, snakes) are highly prevalent, while others (e.g., electricity) are rare.
* **Evolutionary Preparedness vs. Cultural Influence:** Seligman's "evolutionary preparedness" hypothesis suggests humans have a biological readiness to fear objects that threatened prehistoric man. Alternatively, cultural factors (e.g., negative connotations of spiders) may explain this selectivity.
* **Subtypes:** DSM-IV identifies four main types: animal, natural environment (e.g., heights), blood-injection-injury, and situational (e.g., claustrophobia). A miscellaneous "other" category exists (e.g., choking phobia).

**The Three-Systems Model of Fear (Lang, 1968):**
Emotions consist of three relatively independent components:
1. **Physiological:** Autonomic symptoms (tachycardia, increased respiration). Measured via psychophysiology (heart rate, SCR).
2. **Subjective:** Feelings of apprehension or disgust. Measured via self-report (e.g., Spider Phobia Questionnaire).
3. **Behavioural:** Avoidance or escape. Measured by standardised approach tasks.
*Note:* Different phobias have different profiles. Animal phobia features fear, whereas blood-injection-injury phobia is strongly associated with disgust and repulsion. Situational phobias involve more complex cognitions (e.g., fear of going crazy) compared to animal phobias.

**Irrational Nature and Neural Pathways:**
Phobics recognize their fear is excessive but cannot control it. This may be due to fast, subcortical information processing (the thalamo-amygdala pathway described by LeDoux) that triggers a fear response before conscious recognition.

### 6.2 Epidemiology, Natural Course, and Genetics
* **Prevalence:** Highly common. NCS lifetime prevalence is 11.3%. Animal fears are the most prevalent.
* **Gender:** Diagnosed more often in women than men, especially animal phobias. This is not fully explained by social permissibility and may involve predisposing factors like sex hormones.
* **Natural Course:** Mild fears are common in childhood and often transient. However, clinical specific phobias are chronic; they rarely remit without treatment and tend to become more severe over time.
* **Genetics:** Family and twin studies suggest a genetic contribution. A "general" (higher-order) genetic factor contributes to broad temperamental traits (neuroticism, negative affectivity) acting as a vulnerability for a wide range of phobias. "Specific" (lower-order) genetic factors predispose to circumscribed fears (more important for situational/social phobias than animal phobias). Comorbidity between phobia subtypes is high.

### 6.3 Developmental Aspects
* **Age of Onset:** Generally early. Mean onset is 7 years for animal phobia and 9 years for blood-injury-injection phobia. Heights fear often has an early or lifelong onset. In contrast, situational phobias (claustrophobia) have a later onset (mean 20 years).
* **Childhood Fears:** Mild fears are normal (peaking at ages 7-9) and fluctuate with cognitive development. 
* **Behavioural Inhibition:** Kagan identified "behavioural inhibition" (shyness, withdrawal from the unfamiliar) in children as a significant vulnerability trait for developing anxiety disorders, including specific phobias, later in life.

### 6.4 Learning Experiences
**Classical Conditioning:**
The traditional Watson & Rayner model ("Little Albert") posits that pairing a neutral stimulus (CS) with an aversive event (UCS) creates a conditioned fear response. Mowrer's two-stage theory adds that subsequent avoidance of the CS reduces fear, thereby negatively reinforcing the avoidance behaviour.
* **Shortcomings:** It doesn't explain the non-random distribution of fears, instances of trauma without subsequent phobia (due to "latent inhibition" from prior non-aversive exposure), or phobias without preceding trauma.

**The Neoconditioning Approach (S-S Learning):**
Views conditioning as a cognitive process where the subject learns the CS predicts the UCS. It incorporates:
* **Latent Inhibition:** Prior safe exposure to a stimulus protects against later fear conditioning.
* **UCS Inflation:** A mild aversive event is paired with a CS; later, independent information inflates the perceived severity of the UCS, causing the CS to elicit severe fear.

**Indirect Pathways (Rachman's Three-Pathway Model):**
1. **Modelling:** Acquiring fear by observing others (e.g., parents) react fearfully.
2. **Negative Information:** Acquiring fear through verbal warnings or cultural transmission. This is strongly linked to "disgust sensitivity," which heavily influences small animal phobias.
*Retrospective studies show that while conditioning is frequently reported for blood-injury and situational phobias, modelling and negative information are heavily implicated in animal phobias.*

### 6.5 Cognitive Biases
Specific phobias are maintained by highly restricted, content-dependent cognitive biases:
* **Attentional Bias:** Hyperattention to threat-related stimuli (demonstrated by the modified Stroop colour task). High trait anxious individuals automatically prioritize threat processing.
* **Co-variation Bias:** The tendency to overestimate the association between phobic stimuli and aversive outcomes.
* **Ex Consequentia Inference:** The belief that experiencing anxiety symptoms inherently implies the presence of objective danger.

### 6.6 Treatment
* **Exposure In Vivo:** The treatment of choice. Involves graded, prolonged confrontation with the phobic object while preventing avoidance. Highly effective (74-94% success for one-session treatments).
* **Mechanisms:** Exposure likely works by changing cognitive structures (correcting catastrophic misinterpretations) rather than simple habituation.
* **Alternatives:** EMDR has been promoted but controlled studies show it is inferior to exposure therapy; its active ingredient appears to be the exposure element itself.
* **Pharmacotherapy:** No specific drug is recommended. Benzodiazepines may decrease subjective fear but do not improve approach behaviour and may cause state-dependency effects, impeding true learning.

## 7. Social Phobia (Social Anxiety Disorder)
**Authors:** (Standard Clinical Synthesis based on the compendium text)

### 7.1 Introduction and Historical Context
Historically, severe social anxiety was often subsumed under other diagnostic categories or viewed as a severe personality trait (e.g., avoidant personality). It gained formal, independent diagnostic status in DSM-III (1980) as Social Phobia. DSM-IV further refined the criteria, emphasizing the core feature: a marked and persistent fear of social or performance situations in which the person is exposed to unfamiliar people or to possible scrutiny by others. The individual fears they will act in a way (or show anxiety symptoms) that will be humiliating or embarrassing.

### 7.2 Clinical Picture and Symptomatology
The core cognitive distortion in Social Phobia is the fear of negative evaluation. 

**Subtypes:**
* **Generalised Social Phobia:** The fear encompasses most social interactions and public situations (e.g., initiating conversations, participating in small groups, dating, interacting with authority figures). This subtype is generally more severe, highly debilitating, and strongly overlaps with Avoidant Personality Disorder.
* **Non-Generalised (Specific) Social Phobia:** The fear is restricted to one or a few specific performance situations, most commonly public speaking or performing in front of an audience, while routine social interactions are unimpaired.

**Somatic and Autonomic Symptoms:**
Unlike Panic Disorder, where the fear is often of the physical symptoms themselves (fear of dying/heart attack), the somatic symptoms in Social Phobia are feared because they might be *visible* to others, leading to judgment.
* Blushing (highly specific to social phobia).
* Trembling or shaking (especially hands or voice).
* Sweating.
* Gastrointestinal distress or nausea.

### 7.3 Epidemiology and Course
* **Prevalence:** Once considered rare, epidemiological studies (like the NCS) have revealed it to be highly prevalent. Lifetime prevalence estimates range from 10% to 13.3%, making it the most common anxiety disorder and the third most common psychiatric disorder overall (behind major depression and alcohol dependence).
* **Gender Ratio:** In epidemiological community samples, women are more frequently affected than men (approx. 1.5:1 to 2:1). However, in clinical treatment-seeking samples, the ratio is often 1:1 or even shows a slight male majority, likely because social role expectations force men to seek treatment for occupational impairments.
* **Age of Onset:** Onset is characteristically early, typically in mid-adolescence (mean age 13–15 years). It frequently emerges out of a childhood history of shyness or behavioural inhibition. Onset after age 25 is relatively rare and often follows a humiliating public event or a drastic change in social roles.
* **Course:** If left untreated, the course is generally chronic, unremitting, and lifelong, leading to severe educational, occupational, and social underachievement.

### 7.4 Comorbidity
Social Phobia is highly comorbid, which often complicates the clinical picture and delays accurate diagnosis.
* **Other Anxiety Disorders:** Frequently co-occurs with Specific Phobias, Panic Disorder, and GAD.
* **Depression:** Highly associated with Major Depressive Disorder and dysthymia. The depression is almost always secondary to the profound isolation, loneliness, and frustration caused by the social phobia.
* **Substance Abuse:** A critical complication. Alcohol abuse/dependence is extraordinarily common (up to 20% - 40% in clinical samples), typically originating as a maladaptive coping mechanism (self-medication) to reduce anticipatory anxiety before social events.

### 7.5 Pathogenesis and Aetiology
* **Genetics and Temperament:** Family studies indicate a moderate genetic risk, particularly for the generalised subtype. The temperamental trait of "behavioural inhibition to the unfamiliar" in infancy and early childhood is a strong predictor of later Social Phobia.
* **Neurobiology:** * *Dopaminergic System:* Some evidence suggests reduced striatal dopamine D2 receptor binding, which may correlate with social detachment and introversion.
    * *Serotonergic System:* Efficacy of SSRIs points to serotonergic involvement, possibly related to hypersensitivity of postsynaptic receptors.
* **Cognitive/Psychological:** Cognitive models emphasize an "attentional bias" where the individual focuses excessively on negative social cues and self-monitoring (internal focus). They also engage in severe "post-event processing" (rumination over perceived social failures).

### 7.6 Treatment
Both psychopharmacology and cognitive-behavioural therapies have demonstrated strong efficacy.

**Pharmacological Treatment:**
* **SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors):** The first-line treatment for generalised social phobia due to their favourable side-effect profile and efficacy against comorbid depression. Paroxetine and sertraline are widely supported by placebo-controlled trials.
* **MAOIs (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors):** Phenelzine is classically considered highly effective (sometimes superior to early SSRIs in specific trials) but is reserved for treatment-resistant cases due to the strict dietary restrictions (tyramine-free diet) and risk of hypertensive crisis.
* **RIMAs (Reversible Inhibitors of MAO-A):** Moclobemide offers a safer alternative to classical MAOIs, though results on its comparative efficacy are mixed.
* **Beta-Blockers:** Propranolol and atenolol are ineffective for generalised social phobia but are highly effective for the *non-generalised/performance* subtype (e.g., musicians, public speakers) when taken PRN (as needed) to block the peripheral manifestations of anxiety (tachycardia, tremor).

**Psychological Treatment:**
* **Cognitive-Behavioural Therapy (CBT):** The psychological treatment of choice. It involves:
    1. *Exposure Therapy:* Graded, systematic in vivo exposure to feared social situations.
    2. *Cognitive Restructuring:* Challenging and modifying maladaptive automatic thoughts and beliefs about social evaluation.
    3. *Social Skills Training (SST):* Sometimes utilized, though most patients have the necessary skills but are inhibited by anxiety from using them. Group CBT is particularly beneficial as the group itself serves as an exposure environment.

## 8. Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
**Authors:** (Standard Clinical Synthesis based on the compendium text)

### 8.1 Introduction and Clinical Picture
Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder is characterized by the presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both, which are severe enough to be time-consuming (taking more than one hour a day) and cause marked distress or significant impairment.
* **Obsessions:** Recurrent, persistent, and intrusive thoughts, impulses, or images that cause marked anxiety or distress. They are ego-dystonic (experienced as alien or inconsistent with the patient's self-concept). The patient attempts to ignore, suppress, or neutralize them.
* **Compulsions:** Repetitive behaviours (e.g., hand washing, ordering, checking) or mental acts (e.g., praying, counting, repeating words silently) that the person feels driven to perform in response to an obsession or according to rigidly applied rules. The goal is to prevent or reduce distress, though the acts are not connected in a realistic way to what they are designed to neutralize, or are clearly excessive.

*Note on Insight:* Most patients recognise that their obsessions or compulsions are excessive or unreasonable at some point during the illness, though a "poor insight" specifier is recognized when the patient is convinced the obsessive beliefs are true.

### 8.2 Phenomenology and Subtypes
OCD manifests in highly heterogeneous ways, but typically falls into several distinct dimensions or symptom clusters:
* **Contamination/Washing:** Obsessions about dirt, germs, or contamination leading to compulsive washing and cleaning. Avoidance of "contaminated" objects is severe.
* **Doubt/Checking:** Pathological doubt (e.g., "Did I lock the door?", "Did I hit a pedestrian?") leading to compulsive checking to prevent an imagined disaster.
* **Symmetry/Ordering:** A need for objects to be perfectly aligned or tasks performed in a "just right" manner, often leading to time-consuming ordering or repeating rituals.
* **Unacceptable/Taboo Thoughts:** Intrusive aggressive, sexual, or religious (scrupulosity) thoughts, often accompanied by mental rituals (e.g., silently repeating a phrase to neutralize a "bad" thought).
* **Hoarding:** Historically grouped within OCD (and frequently co-occurring), characterized by the inability to discard worn-out or worthless items, leading to severe clutter.

### 8.3 Epidemiology and Course
* **Prevalence:** Once thought to be a rare psychological curiosity, epidemiological studies (e.g., ECA) have identified OCD as a "hidden epidemic." The lifetime prevalence is consistently estimated at 2% to 3% across diverse international populations.
* **Gender Ratio:** Generally equal between males and females in adulthood (1:1). However, males tend to have an earlier age of onset.
* **Age of Onset:** Typically begins in adolescence or early adulthood. Mean age of onset for males is 13–15 years, and for females is 20–24 years.
* **Course:** Without treatment, the course is usually chronic and fluctuating, with exacerbations frequently tied to environmental stressors. Spontaneous complete remission is highly unusual.

### 8.4 Comorbidity
OCD is highly comorbid with other psychiatric conditions, which can complicate treatment.
* **Depression:** Major Depressive Disorder is the most frequent comorbid condition (occurring in up to 67% of cases over a lifetime). Depression usually occurs *secondary* to the distress and impairment caused by the OCD.
* **Tic Disorders:** Up to 20-30% of OCD patients have a lifetime history of a tic disorder, particularly in males with childhood-onset OCD. This "tic-related OCD" may have distinct genetic and pharmacological profiles.
* **Other Anxiety Disorders:** Panic Disorder, Social Phobia, and Specific Phobias frequently co-occur.
* **OCD-Spectrum Disorders:** Strong overlap with Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD), Trichotillomania (hair-pulling), and Hypochondriasis.

### 8.5 Pathogenesis and Aetiology
* **Neurobiological/Anatomical:** Neuroimaging (PET/fMRI) consistently shows hyperactivity in the Cortico-Striato-Thalamo-Cortical (CSTC) circuit—specifically the orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate gyrus, and caudate nucleus. Successful treatment (both pharmacological and CBT) normalizes this hyperactivity.
* **Neurotransmitter Systems:** The Serotonergic hypothesis is dominant, driven by the fact that *only* potent Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SRIs) are effective, whereas primarily noradrenergic antidepressants are not. Dopaminergic involvement is also suspected, especially in tic-related OCD.
* **Autoimmune Factors (PANDAS):** In a subset of children, an explosive onset of OCD or tics is triggered by a Group A beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection, leading to an autoimmune cross-reaction affecting the basal ganglia.
* **Cognitive-Behavioural Models:** * *Mowrer's Two-Stage Theory:* Obsessions generate anxiety (classical conditioning), and compulsions relieve that anxiety, reinforcing the compulsion (operant conditioning/negative reinforcement).
    * *Salkovskis' Cognitive Theory:* Emphasizes "Thought-Action Fusion" (the belief that having a thought is morally equivalent to acting on it, or increases the likelihood of it happening) and "Inflated Responsibility" (the belief that one has pivotal power to bring about or prevent crucial negative outcomes). 

### 8.6 Treatment
A combined approach or robust monotherapy using psychopharmacology or psychological interventions is standard.

**Pharmacological Treatment:**
* **SRIs and SSRIs:** The first-line pharmacological treatment. Clomipramine (a tricyclic with potent serotonergic activity) is historically the gold standard. However, SSRIs (fluvoxamine, fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline, citalopram) are preferred due to a much safer side-effect profile.
* *Crucial Difference from Depression:* OCD requires significantly **higher doses** of SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine up to 80mg) and a **longer trial period** (10 to 12 weeks) before evaluating efficacy compared to Major Depression.
* **Augmentation:** For partial responders, augmenting the SSRI with a low-dose atypical antipsychotic (e.g., risperidone, haloperidol) is common, particularly for tic-related OCD.

**Psychological Treatment:**
* **Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP):** The psychological treatment of choice and highly efficacious. It involves exposing the patient to the anxiety-provoking stimulus (e.g., touching a public doorknob) while strictly preventing the performance of the compulsive ritual (e.g., hand washing). This allows the anxiety to naturally habituate and breaks the negative reinforcement cycle.
* **Cognitive Therapy:** Often used alongside ERP to directly challenge the cognitive distortions of inflated responsibility, overestimation of threat, and thought-action fusion.

**Neurosurgical Treatment:**
* For severely debilitating, highly treatment-refractory cases, stereotactic neurosurgery (e.g., anterior capsulotomy, cingulotomy) may be considered, targeting the overactive CSTC pathways.

## 9. Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder: Biology and Treatment, a Generation of Progress
**Authors:** I. Iancu, Y. Sasson, N. Nakash, M. Chopra and J. Zohar (Sheba Medical Center, Tel Hashomer, Israel)

### 9.1 Introduction
For many years, Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) was considered a rare, psychologically driven, and largely untreatable condition. Over the past generation, however, an explosion of research has revolutionized our understanding of the disorder. The discovery of specific antiobsessional pharmacological agents, advancements in neuroimaging, and the development of targeted cognitive-behavioral therapies have shifted the paradigm to view OCD as a neurobiological disorder with highly effective, specific treatments.

### 9.2 Biological Models of OCD

#### 9.2.1 The Serotonin Hypothesis
The most robust biological hypothesis for OCD implicates a dysregulation of the serotonergic (5-HT) system.
* **Pharmacological Evidence:** The primary basis for this hypothesis is that *only* potent Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SRIs)—such as clomipramine and the Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)—are effective in reducing OCD symptoms. Antidepressants lacking potent serotonergic activity (e.g., desipramine, maprotiline) do not demonstrate antiobsessional efficacy.
* **Challenge Studies:** Administration of m-chlorophenylpiperazine (mCPP), a partial 5-HT agonist, has been shown to temporarily exacerbate obsessive-compulsive symptoms in some patients, suggesting altered sensitivity of post-synaptic 5-HT receptors.

#### 9.2.2 Neuroimaging Findings
Neuroimaging techniques (PET, SPECT, fMRI) have provided compelling evidence of specific regional brain abnormalities in OCD patients.
* **The CSTC Circuit:** Abnormalities are consistently localized to the Cortico-Striato-Thalamo-Cortical (CSTC) circuit. 
* **Hypermetabolism:** Studies routinely reveal increased glucose metabolism and blood flow in the **orbitofrontal cortex**, the **caudate nucleus**, and the **anterior cingulate gyrus** in symptomatic OCD patients.
* **Treatment Correlates:** Successful treatment with either SSRIs or Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) has been shown to normalize this hypermetabolism, establishing a direct link between the resolution of symptoms and the dampening of hyperactivity within the CSTC circuit.

#### 9.2.3 Other Biological Pathways
* **Dopaminergic System:** The high rate of comorbidity with Tourette's syndrome and tic disorders suggests dopaminergic involvement. In a subset of patients with "tic-related OCD," serotonergic drugs alone are often insufficient, and augmentation with dopamine antagonists (neuroleptics) is required.
* **Autoimmune Subtype (PANDAS):** Pediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Streptococcal infections. A subset of childhood-onset OCD is triggered by Group A beta-hemolytic streptococcal infections, where cross-reactive antibodies attack the basal ganglia, leading to an abrupt onset of obsessions, compulsions, and tics.

### 9.3 Pharmacological Treatment
Pharmacotherapy has transformed the prognosis for OCD patients.

#### 9.3.1 First-Line Agents: SSRIs and Clomipramine
* **Clomipramine (CMI):** A tricyclic antidepressant with potent serotonin reuptake inhibition. It was the first drug demonstrated to possess specific antiobsessional properties, independent of its antidepressant effects.
* **SSRIs:** Fluoxetine, fluvoxamine, paroxetine, sertraline, and citalopram are now considered the first-line treatments. Large multicenter, placebo-controlled trials have established their efficacy.
* **The "OCD Rule":** Treating OCD pharmacologically requires a fundamentally different approach than treating depression:
    1. **Higher Doses:** Maximum tolerated doses are often required (e.g., fluoxetine up to 80 mg/day, fluvoxamine up to 300 mg/day).
    2. **Longer Latency:** While antidepressants typically take 3-4 weeks to show effects, SRIs in OCD require a trial period of **10 to 12 weeks** before treatment failure can be declared.

#### 9.3.2 Treatment Resistance and Augmentation
Approximately 40-60% of patients have an inadequate response to an initial SRI trial. Strategies for treatment-resistant OCD include:
* **Switching:** Trying a different SSRI or switching to clomipramine.
* **Augmentation:** * *Antipsychotics:* Adding low-dose typical (haloperidol) or atypical (risperidone) neuroleptics is highly effective, specifically for patients with comorbid tics or schizotypal features.
    * *Other Agents:* Clonazepam, buspirone, or lithium have shown varied efficacy as augmenters.
    * *Intravenous Clomipramine:* Has shown rapid symptom reduction in some refractory cases, bypassing first-pass hepatic metabolism.

### 9.4 Psychological Treatment: Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
CBT, specifically Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP), is the psychological treatment of choice.

* **Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP):** Based on learning theory (Mowrer's two-stage model). It involves prolonged, deliberate exposure to obsessional triggers (e.g., dirt) while strictly preventing the accompanying compulsive ritual (e.g., hand-washing). This breaks the cycle of negative reinforcement and leads to physiological habituation of the anxiety.
* **Efficacy:** ERP is highly effective (often matching or exceeding pharmacotherapy) and has lower relapse rates than medication upon discontinuation.
* **Cognitive Interventions:** Focus on correcting cognitive distortions such as "overestimation of threat" and "inflated responsibility" (the belief that one has the power to bring about or prevent crucial negative outcomes).

### 9.5 Combined Treatment and Future Directions
The combination of ERP and an SSRI is widely considered the gold standard for moderate to severe OCD. For the most debilitating, profoundly refractory cases (less than 1%), neurosurgical interventions (such as stereotactic anterior capsulotomy or cingulotomy) may be considered, targeting the hyperactive pathways connecting the frontal lobes to the basal ganglia.

## 10. Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
**Authors:** N. Caycedo and E.J.L. Griez (Barcelona, Spain and Maastricht University, Maastricht, The Netherlands)

### 10.1 Introduction: About a Disputed Concept
The conceptualization of Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD) has evolved significantly over the past century. Initially subsumed under Kraepelin's (1896) "psychogenic neurosis" and Freud's "anxiety neurosis," chronic anxiety was often viewed as a mere symptom rather than an independent disease. 

In the 1960s, Klein proposed subdividing Freud's anxiety neurosis into "panic attacks" (paroxysmal bursts) and chronic, generalised anxiety, based on differing pharmacosensitivity. This led to the Research Diagnostic Criteria (RDC) in 1975, which first mentioned GAD as a separate entity. 

**Evolution of Diagnostic Criteria:**
* **DSM-III (1980):** GAD was defined as a generalised, persistent feeling of anxiety lasting at least one month. It required symptoms from three of four categories: motor tension, autonomic hyperactivity, apprehensive expectation, and vigilance/scanning. GAD was largely considered a "residual" category, excluded if criteria for another Axis I disorder were met.
* **DSM-III-R (1987):** Elevated GAD to a primary diagnostic category. The central focus shifted to "apprehensive expectation" and "worry" (excessive/unrealistic and not circumscribed to one life circumstance). Duration was extended to six months. Patients needed six of 18 somatic symptoms. 
* **DSM-IV (1994):** Increased emphasis on "uncontrollable worry" (excessive, pervasive, and difficult to control). The somatic symptom criteria were drastically reduced to only six symptoms, entirely eliminating the autonomic hyperactivity cluster, thereby solidifying GAD as a highly "cognitive" condition.

### 10.2 Prevalence
Owing to shifting diagnostic criteria, GAD prevalence rates have varied. 
* **National Comorbidity Survey (NCS, USA):** Using DSM-III-R criteria, the NCS found a 1.6% current prevalence (most recent six months), 3.1% 12-month prevalence, and 5.1% lifetime prevalence. These rates make GAD more common than panic disorder in the general population.
* **Epidemiological Catchment Area (ECA) Study:** Reported a 12-month prevalence of 3% and a lifetime prevalence of 4-6%.
* **Elderly Populations:** GAD shows higher prevalence rates than any other anxiety disorder, ranging from 0.7% to 7.1%.
* **Clinical Settings:** GAD is the most frequently diagnosed anxiety disorder in primary care centers, although it frequently goes unrecognised or underdiagnosed due to high psychiatric comorbidity. Conversely, in specialized mental health centers, it is one of the least common (only about 10% of anxiety patients treated by psychiatrists have a primary GAD diagnosis).

### 10.3 Course and Demographics
* **Course:** GAD is characterized by extreme anxiety and worry, typically with a chronic course (average duration in studies is 20 years). Stress plays an important role in its onset.
* **Age of Onset:** Typically between the late teens and early twenties. 
    * *Early-onset subgroup:* Patients have "always been anxious," withdrawn, socially sensitive worriers.
    * *Late-onset subgroup:* Better premorbid social adjustment; onset provoked by unfavourable life events.
* **Demographics:** GAD is twice as common in women. Prevalence is higher in urban areas and low-income brackets. Significant correlates for individuals over 24 include being separated, divorced, widowed, unemployed, or a homemaker. 
* **Impairment:** High interference with life is reported by 49% of patients; 66% seek professional help, predominantly in the primary care sector.

### 10.4 Comorbidity
GAD displays exceptionally high psychiatric comorbidity, severely complicating diagnosis and treatment.
* **General Comorbidity:** The ECA study found 58-65% of subjects with GAD have at least one other disorder. The NCS found that 89.9% of subjects with a lifetime GAD diagnosis had at least one other lifetime psychiatric disorder, and 65% of current GAD cases had a comorbid disorder.
* **Specific Overlaps:** The highest comorbidity rates are with Social Phobia (16-59%), Simple Phobia (21-55%), Major Depression (8-39%), and Panic Disorder (3-27%). 

### 10.5 Is GAD an Independent Nosologic Entity?
Because of high comorbidity and symptom overlap, GAD's validity as a separate entity is continually debated. It has been hypothesized to be a mild expression of panic disorder (PD), a residual phase of depression, or an anxious personality disorder. However, several lines of research support its independence:
* **Family Studies:** PD is heavily influenced by hereditary factors, whereas GAD appears more influenced by environmental factors (e.g., life events in early childhood). Noyes et al. (1987, 1992) demonstrated that GAD and PD segregate independently in families (GAD relatives have more GAD; PD relatives have more PD).
* **Clinical Studies:** GAD patients exhibit more hyperarousal symptoms of the central nervous system (sleep difficulties, restlessness, muscle tension, irritability), whereas PD patients report more autonomic hyperactivity (pounding heartbeat, dyspnea, fear of dying). GAD has an earlier, more gradual onset, a more chronic course, and higher social impairment, though patients seek specialized treatment later than PD patients.
* **Challenge Studies:** Unlike PD patients, GAD patients are not vulnerable to lactate infusion. Furthermore, during a 35% $CO_2$ inhalation challenge, GAD patients display a strong autonomic reaction but fail to report the acute increase in panic/anxiety characteristic of PD, suggesting distinctly different underlying biological processes.

### 10.6 Treatment
Because GAD is a chronic disorder, treatment is often intermittent and long-term.

**Pharmacological Treatments:**
* **Benzodiazepines (BDZs):** The historical treatment of choice, producing a rapid response (1-2 weeks) in about 65% of cases. They are highly effective for somatic symptoms of arousal and insomnia but less effective for the "psychic" symptoms (worry, irritability). Major drawbacks include a 65% recurrence rate upon discontinuation and high risk of physical dependence and withdrawal. (Risk factors for severe withdrawal include high dosage, short half-life, rapid taper, axis II pathology, and history of drug/tobacco dependence).
* **Azapirones (Buspirone):** An effective anxiolytic that takes 2-4 weeks to act. It yields a slight antidepressant effect and is highly effective for "psychic symptoms" (obsessive worry and ruminations). It is the preferred choice when avoiding withdrawal symptoms and drug dependence is a priority.
* **Antidepressants:** Imipramine and trazodone have proven efficacy within 2-4 weeks, successfully treating psychic anxiety symptoms alongside comorbid depression. Newer agents like venlafaxine and nefazodone are also effective and offer better tolerability.

**Psychotherapy:**
* **Cognitive-Behaviour Therapy (CBT):** The most promising psychological intervention. It involves cognitive restructuring (e.g., "downward arrow" technique, probability revaluation), behaviour therapy, and applied relaxation (e.g., Jacobson's, Schultz's autogenic training, Caycedian sophrology). 
* CBT helps patients alter distorted thinking regarding catastrophic events and intolerance of uncertainty. Long-term studies show that CBT results in maintained gains for up to two years and leads to a substantial reduction in the need for anxiolytic medication.

**Treating Comorbidity:**
Maintenance strategies must account for comorbidities. Co-existing social phobia may suggest the use of MAOIs. If PD is present, antidepressants established for PD should be used. The presence of depressive symptoms strongly predicts a better response to antidepressants or buspirone over benzodiazepines.

### 10.7 Conclusion
While GAD emerged from the shadow of Panic Disorder, its validity has been strengthened by refining diagnostic criteria to focus on uncontrollable cognitive worry. GAD is a highly prevalent condition, particularly in primary care. Recognizing GAD is critical not just for immediate relief, but because it often serves as a prodromal phase or forerunner to more severe, incapacitating psychopathology. Early intervention in primary care may prevent the development of these severe secondary conditions.

## 11. Post-Traumatic Syndromes (PTSD and Acute Stress Disorder)
**Authors:** (Standard Clinical Synthesis based on the compendium text)

### 11.1 Introduction and Diagnostic Evolution
Historically referred to as "shell shock," "combat fatigue," or "traumatic neurosis," Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) was formally introduced into psychiatric nosology in DSM-III (1980). This was a paradigm shift because it linked the etiology of a psychiatric disorder to a specific, identifiable external event (Criterion A) rather than internal psychological conflicts or purely biological predispositions. 

**Diagnostic Distinction:**
* **Acute Stress Disorder (ASD):** Introduced in DSM-IV to describe severe acute reactions occurring within the first month after the trauma (lasting a minimum of 2 days and a maximum of 4 weeks). It emphasizes dissociative symptoms (numbing, depersonalisation, derealisation, dissociative amnesia).
* **PTSD:** Diagnosed only when symptoms persist for more than one month. It can be specified as *Acute* (duration < 3 months), *Chronic* (duration > 3 months), or *With Delayed Onset* (symptoms begin at least 6 months after the stressor).

### 11.2 Symptomatology and Clinical Picture
PTSD is characterized by three distinct symptom clusters that follow the traumatic exposure:
1. **Re-experiencing (Intrusions):** The trauma is persistently re-experienced through recurrent distressing recollections, nightmares, or dissociative flashback episodes where the individual acts or feels as if the event were recurring. Psychological and physiological reactivity to cues resembling the trauma is intense.
2. **Avoidance and Emotional Numbing:** Active avoidance of thoughts, feelings, conversations, places, or people associated with the trauma. This is coupled with a general "numbing" of responsiveness: inability to recall key aspects of the trauma, markedly diminished interest in activities, feelings of detachment or estrangement from others, and a restricted range of affect (e.g., inability to have loving feelings).
3. **Hyperarousal:** Persistent symptoms of increased autonomic arousal not present before the trauma, including insomnia, irritability or outbursts of anger, difficulty concentrating, hypervigilance, and an exaggerated startle response.

### 11.3 Epidemiology and Course
* **Trauma Exposure vs. PTSD Development:** Exposure to traumatic events is highly common in the general population (estimated at 50% to 60% lifetime exposure). However, only a minority of those exposed develop PTSD, indicating significant individual vulnerability factors.
* **Prevalence:** The National Comorbidity Survey (NCS) estimates the lifetime prevalence of PTSD at 7.8% in the general US population.
* **Gender Differences:** PTSD is approximately twice as common in women (10.4% lifetime prevalence) as in men (5.0%). This discrepancy persists even when controlling for the types of trauma experienced, although women are statistically more likely to experience sexual assault (which carries a very high PTSD risk), while men are more likely to experience combat or physical assault.
* **Natural Course:** Symptoms typically begin immediately after the trauma, but the course is highly variable. Approximately 50% of cases spontaneously remit within the first 3 to 6 months. For the remaining individuals, the disorder often becomes chronic and can persist for decades if untreated.

### 11.4 Risk Factors and Vulnerability
Not everyone exposed to a severe stressor develops PTSD. Predictive vulnerability factors include:
* **Pre-trauma Factors:** Female gender, lower socio-economic status, lower educational attainment, prior psychiatric history (especially anxiety or depression), and importantly, a history of prior trauma (e.g., childhood physical or sexual abuse).
* **Peri-traumatic Factors:** The severity, duration, and proximity of the trauma. The strongest clinical predictor of later PTSD development is the degree of **peri-traumatic dissociation** (how detached or "unreal" the person felt *during* the event).
* **Post-trauma Factors:** Lack of robust social support systems post-event is heavily correlated with chronicity.

### 11.5 Neurobiology of PTSD
PTSD features distinct neurobiological anomalies that differentiate it from general anxiety or major depression:
* **HPA Axis Dysregulation:** Unlike Major Depression (characterized by elevated cortisol), PTSD is paradoxically associated with **hypocortisolism** (lower than normal resting cortisol levels) combined with hypersensitive glucocorticoid receptors. This results in an exaggerated negative feedback loop.
* **Adrenergic Hyperactivity:** Elevated baseline sympathetic nervous system arousal, evidenced by increased urinary catecholamines and exaggerated cardiovascular responses to startle.
* **Neuroanatomical Changes:** MRI studies consistently show **reduced hippocampal volume** in chronic PTSD patients. The hippocampus is vital for contextualizing memories; its dysfunction may explain why trauma memories intrude as fragmented, current-tense flashbacks rather than past contextualized narratives. 
* **Amygdala/mPFC Dysfunction:** Functional imaging shows amygdala hyperactivity (exaggerated threat detection) combined with hypoactivity in the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), indicating a failure of higher cortical centers to inhibit the primitive fear response.

### 11.6 Comorbidity
Comorbidity in PTSD is the rule rather than the exception.
* Over 80% of individuals with PTSD meet criteria for at least one other psychiatric disorder.
* **Major Depressive Disorder:** The most frequent comorbid diagnosis (nearly 50% lifetime overlap).
* **Substance Abuse/Dependence:** Extremely common, particularly alcohol dependence, which often begins as an attempt to "self-medicate" hyperarousal and intrusive symptoms.
* **Other Anxiety Disorders:** High rates of co-occurring Panic Disorder and Generalized Anxiety Disorder.

### 11.7 Treatment
Effective treatment usually involves specialized, trauma-focused interventions.

**Psychological Treatment:**
* **Trauma-Focused CBT:** The gold standard of treatment. **Prolonged Exposure (PE)** therapy (developed by Edna Foa) involves systematic, repeated imaginal exposure to the traumatic memory and in vivo exposure to avoided trauma reminders, leading to habituation and cognitive restructuring.
* **Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT):** Focuses heavily on identifying and challenging "stuck points"—maladaptive beliefs about safety, trust, power/control, esteem, and intimacy that were altered by the trauma.
* **EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing):** Demonstrated efficacy, though the active mechanism is heavily debated. Most empirical evidence suggests the therapeutic effect comes from the imaginal exposure element rather than the bilateral eye movements themselves.

**Pharmacological Treatment:**
* **SSRIs:** The first-line pharmacological agents. Sertraline and paroxetine possess specific FDA indications for PTSD. They are effective for intrusive symptoms, avoidance, and comorbid depression, though less effective for the core hyperarousal symptoms compared to CBT.
* **Prazosin:** An alpha-1 adrenergic antagonist specifically used off-label to effectively treat trauma-related nightmares and sleep disruptions by dampening nocturnal noradrenergic storms.
* **Benzodiazepines:** Generally contraindicated in PTSD. While they may temporarily reduce anxiety, they can impede the fear-extinction learning required for recovery and carry a high risk of abuse in this vulnerable population.

## 12. The Psychobiology of Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder
**Author:** W.S. de Loos (Central Military Hospital, Utrecht, The Netherlands)

### 12.1 Neuroanatomy and Brain Area Function
Important findings over the past decade highlight that traumatic experiences change the brain not only functionally, but also anatomically at submicroscopic, microscopic, and gross anatomical levels.
* **Limbic System & Amygdala:** Critical for comparing sensory input to stored memory and organizing psychological and physiological output. Severe, overwhelming stress input may cause loss of synaptic connections and even cell death.
* **Hippocampal Changes:** Several studies report decreased hippocampal volume (particularly on the right side) in PTSD patients. This structural decrease is associated with functional deficits in verbal (declarative) memory and context-dependent memory processing.
* **Broca's Area and Alexithymia:** Functional neuroimaging shows increased activity in the right amygdala coupled with *decreased* activation in Broca's area. This suggests a neurological basis for the decreased capacity to translate traumatic experiences into communicable language, supporting the nearly obligatory clinical observation of "alexithymia" (the inability to express emotions effectively) in PTSD patients.
* **Psychophysiological Dimensions:** The human stress response spans multiple dimensions: 
    * *Biological/Psychophysiological:* Common to all species; the basic physiology of adaptation.
    * *Pathophysiological:* Defective functioning that becomes irreversible.
    * *Associative/Dissociative:* Avoidance linked to specific memories (conversion) or dissociation. 
    * *Semantic/Declarative:* Specifically human; the ability to declare emotions and learn from psychophysiological sensations.

### 12.2 Psychological and Behavioural Aspects: Predictors and Risk Factors

#### Genetic Factors
Genetic factors contribute substantially to the susceptibility to PTSD symptomatology, although PTSD is not a single-gene disorder.
* **Twin Studies:** Comparisons of monozygotic (MZ) and dizygotic (DZ) twins demonstrate that heredity contributes to nearly all PTSD symptoms. While the *level* of combat exposure predicts re-experiencing and avoidance, shared family-life experiences do not significantly contribute to PTSD susceptibility.
* **Dopaminergic System:** A polymorphism of the dopamine D2-receptor gene (the D2A1 allele) is highly associated with PTSD, particularly the hyperarousal criteria. This same allele is prevalent in ADHD, Tourette's syndrome, conduct disorder, and alcoholism, suggesting a shared, genetically determined biological floorplan that affects human stress responses across multiple diagnostic categories. 

#### Psychological Factors and Interactions
* **Vulnerability to Exposure:** Traits like neuroticism and extroversion prospectively predict exposure to traumatic events. MZ twins show higher concordance for volunteering for military service and combat exposure than DZ twins, indicating genetic influences on the likelihood of encountering trauma.
* **Gender:** Women are more likely to develop PTSD after major trauma, and their symptoms tend to last longer, even after adjusting for pre-trauma physical/sexual abuse and psychiatric history. However, prior abuse strongly promotes PTSD development following later trauma (e.g., combat).
* **Specific vs. General Vulnerability:** In WWII veterans, combat exposure and peri-traumatic physiological symptoms predicted PTSD up to 41 years later. In contrast, psychosocial vulnerability during adolescence predicted later trait neuroticism, showing that combat exposure is specifically linked to PTSD, while premorbid vulnerability is linked to general psychoneurotic pathology.

#### Early Signs as Predictors
Clinicians can identify subjects at high risk for PTSD by observing the acute post-trauma phase:
* **Peri-Traumatic Dissociation:** The degree of dissociation experienced *during* or immediately after the trauma is an even stronger predictor of PTSD development than early depression, anxiety, or intrusive symptoms.
* **Physiological Markers:** High heart rate in the emergency room (peri-traumatic phase) positively correlates with later PTSD, intrusive symptoms, and exaggerated startle.
* **Cortisol Levels:** A negative correlation exists between serum cortisol immediately after traumatization (e.g., sexual assault, motor vehicle accidents) and the development of PTSD, pointing to pre-traumatic neuroendocrine vulnerabilities. 
* **Critical Period:** While acute stress responses strongly predict PTSD, the first three months following the trauma appear to be the critical window for the full disorder to solidify.

### 12.3 Lifetime Development and Course
The long-term trajectory of PTSD is not uniform and exhibits several distinct patterns:
* **Acute to Chronic:** Initial acute symptoms gradually crystallize into a chronic condition.
* **Delayed Onset:** Clinical manifestation may be delayed for a considerable number of years, sometimes up to three or four decades.
* **Relapsing Course:** An early manifestation may be followed by a symptom-free interval spanning years or decades, only to relapse later.
* **Triggering Events in Later Life:** PTSD of substantial duration is rarely the consequence of just one isolated event. Often, a recent stressor triggers unconscious or repressed memories of significant earlier traumatization. Conditions that can acutely trigger or exacerbate PTSD in later life include:
    * Intrusive medical treatments or hospitalizations (which may act as a sensory reminder of trauma, e.g., a concentration camp sick barrack).
    * Retirement or drop-out from the workforce (loss of structure/distraction).
    * The general ageing process.

## 13. Learning Perspectives on Anxiety Disorders
**Authors:** P. Eelen, D. Hermans, and F. Baeyens (University of Leuven, Belgium)

### 13.1 Introduction
The application of learning theory to the understanding and treatment of anxiety disorders has undergone significant evolution. Early behaviourist models relied on mechanistic views of conditioning, while modern learning paradigms incorporate cognitive, information-processing frameworks. Learning perspectives remain central to the etiology of anxiety disorders and form the theoretical backbone of exposure-based therapies.

### 13.2 Classical (Pavlovian) Conditioning and Fear Acquisition
The foundational model for acquired fear is Pavlovian conditioning, famously illustrated by Watson and Rayner's "Little Albert" experiment. 
* **The Mechanism:** A biologically significant, aversive Unconditioned Stimulus (US) elicits an Unconditioned Response (UR) of fear/arousal. When a neutral Conditioned Stimulus (CS) is reliably paired with the US, it acquires the capacity to elicit a Conditioned Response (CR) of fear.
* **Modern Cognitive Shift (S-S Learning):** Contemporary learning theory has shifted from a mechanistic Stimulus-Response (S-R) model to a Stimulus-Stimulus (S-S) expectancy model. Conditioning is now understood as the learning of relations between events: the CS becomes a *predictor* of the US. Fear is the psychophysiological anticipation of an impending aversive event.

### 13.3 Mowrer's Two-Stage Theory and Operant Conditioning
To explain why phobias and anxiety persist long after the original trauma, Mowrer proposed the Two-Stage Theory:
1. **Stage 1 (Classical Conditioning):** Fear is acquired through the pairing of the CS and US.
2. **Stage 2 (Operant Conditioning):** The individual engages in avoidance or escape behaviours to reduce the anxiety triggered by the CS. This reduction in fear acts as a **negative reinforcer**. 
* **The Vicious Cycle:** Because the individual consistently avoids the CS, they never experience the CS without the US, preventing the natural extinction of the fear response. This strictly maintains disorders like Agoraphobia, Specific Phobias, and OCD (where compulsions act as the avoidance/escape behaviour).

### 13.4 Shortcomings of Traditional Models and Modern Revisions
Traditional classical conditioning cannot explain why some traumas do not lead to phobias, or why some phobias develop without a direct traumatic event. Modern learning theory accounts for this via:
* **Preparedness Theory (Seligman):** Humans possess an evolutionary predisposition to readily associate fear with stimuli that threatened early hominids (e.g., snakes, heights, spiders) compared to modern threats (e.g., electrical outlets). These associations are acquired rapidly and resist extinction.
* **Latent Inhibition:** Prior, safe exposure to a stimulus (CS) significantly retards the acquisition of a fear response if that stimulus is later paired with a trauma (US). This explains why a dog bite does not always result in cynophobia if the victim has a history of positive interactions with dogs.
* **Indirect Fear Acquisition (Rachman's Pathways):** Fear can be conditioned without direct US exposure through *Vicarious Learning* (observing another's fear response) or *Informational Transmission* (receiving threatening information).
* **US-Revaluation / Inflation:** If a person experiences a mild CS-US pairing, and later encounters a highly severe US (even independently), the fear response to the original CS can be retrospectively inflated.

### 13.5 Evaluative Conditioning (EC)
A critical distinction in modern learning research (heavily investigated by the Leuven group) is the difference between *expectancy learning* (traditional Pavlovian fear) and *Evaluative Conditioning* (EC).
* **Mechanism:** EC refers to the transfer of *valence* (liking or disliking). A neutral CS paired with a negative US acquires a negative valence (disgust/dislike) that is independent of the expectation that the US will actually occur.
* **Clinical Implication:** While traditional fear (expectancy) extinguishes when the US no longer follows the CS, evaluative conditioning is highly resistant to extinction. This explains why a spider phobic patient, after successful exposure therapy, no longer believes the spider will harm them (fear extinguished), but still finds the spider deeply repulsive (EC remains). EC is particularly relevant in Blood-Injection-Injury phobia and contamination-based OCD.

### 13.6 Extinction and the Renewal Effect
Extinction is the primary mechanism behind exposure therapy (CBT). 
* **Not Unlearning:** Extinction is not the erasure or "unlearning" of the original CS-US association. Rather, it is the acquisition of a *new, competing* association: the CS now predicts the *absence* of the US.
* **Context Dependency (Bouton):** Because the original fear memory remains intact, extinction is highly context-dependent. The "Renewal Effect" occurs when a patient successfully undergoes extinction in one context (the therapist's office) but experiences a spontaneous return of fear when encountering the CS in a novel or original context. This necessitates conducting exposure therapy across multiple contexts to ensure robust generalisation of the extinction memory.

## 14. Current Trends in Cognitive Behaviour Therapy
**Authors:** (Standard Clinical Synthesis based on the compendium text)

### 14.1 Introduction and Evolution of CBT
Cognitive Behaviour Therapy (CBT) has become the psychological treatment of choice for anxiety disorders, supported by extensive empirical evidence. The evolution of CBT reflects a paradigm shift from purely behaviourist approaches—which relied strictly on conditioning paradigms (habituation and extinction)—to models that emphasize the critical role of information processing and cognitive appraisals. Modern CBT posits that it is not the situation itself that causes anxiety, but the patient's catastrophic interpretation of that situation.

### 14.2 Disorder-Specific Cognitive Models
The most significant current trend in CBT is the move away from generic anxiety management training (e.g., general relaxation) towards highly specific, tailored cognitive models for each anxiety disorder.

* **Panic Disorder (Clark's Model):** Panic attacks result from the catastrophic misinterpretation of normal bodily sensations (e.g., interpreting a benign palpitation as a heart attack). CBT focuses on correcting this misinterpretation via interoceptive exposure (e.g., spinning to induce dizziness, hyperventilating) and cognitive restructuring.
* **Social Phobia (Clark and Wells' Model):** Anxiety is driven by a shift to internal focus (self-focused attention) and the use of the self as a social object. Patients assume others judge their internal sensations as harshly as they do. Treatment involves shifting attention externally and using video feedback to correct distorted self-imagery.
* **Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (Salkovskis' Model):** Intrusive thoughts are normal and universal. OCD pathology develops when a patient applies an *inflated sense of responsibility* to the intrusion (i.e., "If I think about harm coming to my family and don't prevent it, it's my fault"). Treatment targets thought-action fusion directly alongside ERP.
* **Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (Ehlers and Clark's Model):** PTSD occurs when a trauma is processed in a way that produces a sense of *current* threat. This is due to poor elaboration of the trauma memory (fragmented, sensory-heavy rather than contextualized) and negative appraisals of the trauma and its sequelae. Treatment involves updating the trauma memory and changing maladaptive appraisals.

### 14.3 The Role of Safety Behaviours
A crucial advancement in modern CBT is the precise identification and targeting of "safety behaviours" (or safety-seeking behaviours).
* **Definition:** Covert or overt actions taken by a patient to prevent a feared catastrophe (e.g., gripping the steering wheel tightly to prevent a crash during a panic attack; mentally rehearsing sentences in social phobia).
* **Maintenance of Anxiety:** Safety behaviours maintain anxiety because they prevent the disconfirmation of the patient's catastrophic beliefs. If a panic attack passes, the patient attributes their survival to the safety behaviour ("I didn't pass out because I held onto the wall") rather than realizing the sensation was inherently harmless.
* **Clinical Trend:** Modern exposure therapy strictly requires the dropping of safety behaviours during exposure tasks to ensure cognitive change rather than mere physiological habituation.

### 14.4 Behavioural Experiments vs. Habituation
Traditional behaviour therapy viewed exposure simply as a way to "habituate" or exhaust the physiological fear response over time. Current CBT frames exposure as a **Behavioural Experiment**.
* The explicit goal is to test a specific, catastrophic hypothesis. 
* For example, a patient with panic disorder who fears fainting will be asked to hyperventilate while dropping all safety behaviours. The outcome (not fainting) directly falsifies their catastrophic belief, leading to rapid cognitive restructuring and symptom relief, which is often much faster than traditional habituation protocols.

### 14.5 New Delivery Methods and Innovations
To increase accessibility and cost-effectiveness, CBT delivery methods are rapidly expanding beyond the traditional 50-minute clinic hour:
* **Computerized CBT (cCBT) and Internet-Delivered CBT (iCBT):** Programs that guide patients through structured cognitive and behavioural modules, often with minimal therapist contact. Efficacy rates are showing parity with face-to-face therapy for disorders like Panic Disorder and Specific Phobia.
* **Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy (VRET):** Utilizing immersive VR environments to conduct exposure therapy. It is particularly useful for specific phobias (fear of flying, heights) and PTSD (combat simulations), offering a highly controlled bridge between imaginal exposure and in vivo exposure.
* **Pharmacological Augmentation of Learning:** Pioneering research into agents like D-cycloserine (an NMDA receptor partial agonist) to enhance the neurobiological learning and memory consolidation that occurs during extinction (exposure therapy), aiming to make CBT faster and more robust.

## Section B: Methods of Environmental Research

## 15. The Experience Sampling Method in Stress and Anxiety Research
**Authors:** M.W. de Vries, C.I.M. Caes, and P.A.E.G. Delespaul (Maastricht University, The Netherlands)

### 15.1 Introduction to the Experience Sampling Method (ESM)
The Experience Sampling Method (ESM) is a structured diary technique designed to assess subjects in their natural environments. Traditional retrospective clinical questionnaires suffer from severe recall bias, where individuals summarize or distort their past emotional states based on their current mood or cognitive schemas. ESM overcomes this by prompting subjects at random intervals (e.g., via a pre-programmed watch or pager like the SEIKO RC-100) to immediately record their current thoughts, feelings, physical symptoms, and environmental context.

### 15.2 Compliance and Reactivity
A primary methodological concern with ESM is whether the frequent monitoring alters the subject's behavior (reactivity) and whether patients will reliably fill out the assessments (compliance).
* **Compliance:** Compliance to signals has consistently remained at approximately the 75% level across almost all psychiatric disorders. Active psychosis, severe melancholia, and severe obsessive-compulsive disorders pose greater challenges, but compliance remains viable with adequate briefing and debriefing.
* **Reactivity:** Studies show minimal reactivity; patients quickly habituate to the signaling device and return to their baseline behavioral routines.

### 15.3 ESM Findings in Panic Disorder and Agoraphobia
ESM has yielded critical empirical insights that challenge traditional, laboratory-bound or retrospectively assessed theories of agoraphobia.
* **Time Budgets and Context:** Time-allocation data reveals that agoraphobic patients spend significantly more time at home and with family members compared to normal controls or panic patients without agoraphobia. 
* **Challenging Avoidance Theories:** Surprisingly, ESM data indicates that anxiety patients (including agoraphobics) report being in public places *no less often* than subjects with other disorders (like depression or chronic pain). This empirical finding strongly challenges the pure "public avoidance" theories of agoraphobia, suggesting that behavioral treatments relying solely on public desensitization may be misguided.
* **Attachment Theory Support:** Because the primary behavioral difference is the increased time spent in the presence of family members (rather than the absolute avoidance of public spaces), the ESM data provides strong support for social and attachment-based theories of agoraphobia. The presence of a "safe" person is the crucial modifying variable.

### 15.4 Stress, Minor Events, and Neuroendocrine Responses
ESM research has shifted focus from massive, isolated traumatic life events to the cumulative impact of daily hassles and minor stressors.
* **Minor Events and Mood:** ESM results indicate that minor daily events significantly contribute to mood fluctuations within and between days. Minor events involving task demands directly correlate with acute increases in agitation and negative affect.
* **Retrospective Discrepancy:** When comparing ESM momentary responses about a stressful problem with retrospective questionnaires filled out 2.5 days later about the *same* event, there is only modest correspondence, proving the necessity of real-time sampling.
* **Future vs. Past:** Anticipation of *future* events (especially predictable ones) has an even greater effect on momentary mood than reflection on *prior* events.
* **Psychophysiological Links:** Psychosocial stressors and daily activities recorded via ESM have been shown to directly activate neuroendocrine and immunological responses.
* **Cortisol Dynamics:** ESM studies comparing momentary distress with biological markers show that perceived daily stress alters cortisol dynamics. Furthermore, supportive, positively evaluated social contexts can buffer and physiologically correct the negative neuroendocrine effects of stressful events.

### 15.5 Conclusion
ESM provides a unique "microscope" for examining the psychopathology of daily life. By capturing the real-time interaction between environmental contexts, cognitive appraisals, and somatic symptoms, it offers an ecologically valid alternative to retrospective clinical interviews and sterile laboratory challenges.

## Section C: Methods of Pharmacology

## 16. The Pharmacology of Human Anxiety
**Author:** D.J. Nutt (University of Bristol, Bristol, UK)

### 16.1 Introduction and Neurochemical Theories
Understanding the neural bases of anxiety—and specifically the role of neurotransmitters—is a cornerstone of biological psychiatry. Anxiety disorders affect up to 15% of the population and are major risk factors for depression and substance abuse. Historically, theories centered on peripheral autonomic modulators (adrenaline/noradrenaline). Modern neurochemical theories suggest that an increase in either amine or excitatory amino acid (EAA) function leads to anxiety, while a down-regulation of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA-A) inhibitory function is a primary underlying defect. 

**Peptide Neuromodulators:**
* **Cholecystokinin (CCK):** A gut peptide that also functions in the brain. CCK-A receptors regulate eating, whereas CCK-B receptors are heavily implicated in anxiety. CCK-4 administration induces panic.
* **Corticotrophin-Releasing Factor (CRF):** A hypothalamic peptide mediating the stress response. Central injection of CRF produces an arousal state mimicking severe stress (insomnia, impaired eating). CRF antagonists exhibit anxiolytic properties in animal models.
* **Opioids:** Acute stress releases endorphins that suppress anxiety via mu-opioid receptors. Blockade or down-regulation of these receptors (e.g., during opiate withdrawal) precipitates severe anxiety and autonomic arousal.

### 16.2 Neurochemical Approaches to Studying Anxiety
Direct human study relies on multiple indirect biological windows:

* **Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Measures:** CSF levels of noradrenaline are elevated in anxiety disorders and during alcohol withdrawal (a high-anxiety state), correlating directly with symptoms.
* **Neuro-Imaging (PET, SPECT, fMRI):** * *Brain Circuits:* Imaging confirms an "anxiety circuit" involving the limbic system (amygdala, hypothalamus, hippocampus), cingulate, prefrontal cortex, and periaqueductal grey (PAG).
    * *Receptor Binding:* PET ($^{11}C$-flumazenil) and SPECT ($^{123}I$-iomazenil) show a global decrease in GABA-A/benzodiazepine receptor binding in Panic Disorder (PD), particularly in the orbitofrontal cortex, temporal cortex, and insula.
    * *Dopamine Transporters:* SPECT studies report a reduced number of dopamine re-uptake sites in patients with Social Phobia, aligning with findings that low dopamine correlates with anxiety and social withdrawal (e.g., preceding Parkinson's disease).

### 16.3 Pharmacological Challenge Paradigms
A challenge test administers an agent to provoke specific receptor systems, differentiating patients from normal volunteers.
* **Noradrenergic Probes:** Clonidine (an $\alpha_{2}$ agonist that decreases noradrenaline) and yohimbine (an $\alpha_{2}$ antagonist that increases it) reveal **presynaptic noradrenergic hyperreactivity** in PD. Antipanic drugs (TCAs, SSRIs) normalize this hyperreactivity.
* **Serotonergic (5-HT) Probes:** mCPP (a 5-$HT_{2C}$ agonist) induces exaggerated anxiety and restlessness in PD patients. Fenfluramine (a 5-HT releaser) increases baseline anxiety but paradoxically reduces the likelihood of panic, reflecting the fact that different 5-HT subsystems mediate different forms of anxiety (e.g., amygdala/frontal cortex projections increase anxiety, whereas PAG projections inhibit panic).
* **GABA-A / Benzodiazepine Probes:** PD patients show *sub-sensitivity* to benzodiazepine agonists (exhibiting less sedation and less slowing of saccadic eye movements). Conversely, the antagonist flumazenil frequently provokes panic in PD patients.

### 16.4 Experimental Anxiety Production
* **Physiological Models:** 5% or 35% $CO_{2}$ inhalation and hyperventilation trigger panic, highlighting a hyperreactive respiratory center in PD.
* **Psychological Models:** Includes threat of pain (electric shock) or cognitive induction (reading scripts of catastrophic physical events for PD, or embarrassing events for Social Phobia). 
    * *The Cognitive Debate:* Cognitive theorists argue all panic results from catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations. Biological evidence refuting a purely cognitive model includes: panics occurring during non-REM sleep; the inability of peripheral beta-blockers to stop panic; drugs like FG 7142 causing panic beyond conscious control; and the fact that central anxiety often precedes peripheral symptoms in challenge tests.

### 16.5 Universal vs. Selective Anxiogenic Challenges
Anxiogenic challenges are divided into two categories based on patient response:
1. **Universal Challenges:** Provoke anxiety in both normal controls and patients (though patients typically require a lower dose).
    * *Agents:* Pentylenetetrazol (PTZ - GABA chloride channel blocker), FG 7142 (benzodiazepine inverse agonist), high-dose caffeine, CCK-4, yohimbine, and mCPP.
2. **Selective Challenges:** Provoke panic *only* in patients with anxiety disorders (all-or-none response).
    * *Agents:* Sodium lactate, sodium bicarbonate (respiratory/noradrenergic mechanisms), and flumazenil.
* *Note on Antidepressants:* SSRIs and TCAs often act as anxiogenics during the first 1-3 weeks of treatment. Clinical protocols necessitate starting at half-doses to avoid precipitating panic before the long-term therapeutic down-regulation of receptors occurs.

### 16.6 The Central Role of GABA in Anxiety
GABA is the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter (used by 40% of synapses) that prevents excessive excitation.
* **Receptor Blockade:** Pentylenetetrazol (PTZ) blocks the GABA-A chloride channel. Sub-convulsive doses produce profound terror, escape behavior, and long-lasting conditioned avoidance—the three core components of clinical anxiety disorders.
* **Inverse Agonists:** FG 7142 binds to the benzodiazepine site but acts as an inverse agonist, switching off GABA function and causing severe, uncontrollable panic in healthy volunteers.
* **Withdrawal States:** Tolerance to alcohol and benzodiazepines involves a down-regulation of GABA-A receptor function. Sudden withdrawal leaves the brain with a severe deficit of inhibition, resulting in intense anxiety and autonomic storms.
* **Genetic Variability:** The GABA-A receptor consists of 5 sub-units (from a pool of at least 16). Changes in phosphorylation (short-term stress adaptation) or gene expression (long-term tolerance) alter receptor sensitivity. Mice lacking the $\gamma_{2}$ sub-unit exhibit severe, baseline anxiety, proving that genetic alterations in GABA sub-units can directly cause anxious phenotypes.

### 16.7 Therapeutics: Immediate vs. Delayed Anxiolytics
Understanding treatment mechanisms provides insight into pathophysiology:
* **Immediate/Fast-Acting (Minutes-Hours):** Benzodiazepines, barbiturates, and alcohol act immediately by augmenting the GABA-A receptor. Beta-blockers also work quickly but only peripherally, blocking sympathetic activation (useful only in performance anxiety).
* **Delayed-Action (2-6 Weeks):** * TCAs (block 5-HT and NA re-uptake) and MAOIs (block metabolism). 
    * Buspirone (a 5-$HT_{1A}$ partial agonist effective in GAD but not PD).
    * SSRIs and SNRIs are now the primary treatments for PD, OCD, and Social Phobia. Their delayed efficacy relies on the gradual desensitization of post-synaptic receptors rather than the acute increase of synaptic serotonin.

## 17. Clinical Testing of Anxiolytic Drugs
**Author:** M. Bourin (University of Nantes, Nantes, France)

### 17.1 Introduction and Diagnostic Targets
Developing and testing new anxiolytic drugs presents significant methodological challenges. Because anxiety disorders have been dissected into distinct entities (DSM-IV and ICD-10), the broad indication of "anxiety" is no longer acceptable for drug trials. While SSRIs and TCAs are used for Panic Disorder, Social Phobia, and OCD, the clinical testing for new, general anxiolytic drugs almost inevitably targets:
* **Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD):** Diagnosed by exclusion. The DSM-IV criteria require excessive worry lasting at least six months. 
* **Adjustment Disorder with Anxious Mood (ADAM):** A maladaptive reaction to an identifiable psychosocial stressor. 

**Methodological Hurdles:**
* *Comorbidity:* "Pure" GAD without associated pathology is rare. Dysthymia and major depression are frequently comorbid, complicating patient selection.
* *Gold Standard Bias:* Benzodiazepines (BZD) are the historical gold standard, and the most common clinical evaluation tools (like the HAM-A) were developed based on BZD symptom profiles, which may not be appropriate for testing new, non-BZD anxiolytics.

### 17.2 Phase I Studies (Healthy Volunteers)
Phase I studies evaluate tolerance, toxicity, and basic pharmacokinetics using healthy, non-anxious volunteers. A primary goal is to map the drug's effects on cognition and psychomotor function, as BZDs classically produce sedation, psychomotor impairment, and anterograde amnesia.

**Standard Psychometric Battery:**
1. **Free Recall of Pictures:** Tests anterograde amnesia. Subjects memorize 20 pictures and recall them at 30-second intervals.
2. **Digit Symbol Substitution Test (DSST):** Adapted from the WAIS. Tests psychomotor speed by substituting symbols for digits within 90 seconds.
3. **Choice Reaction Time (CRT):** Uses the Leeds Psychomotor Tester (LPT) to assess sensorimotor performance via light-extinguishing tasks. It measures Recognition Reaction Time (RRT) and Motor Reaction Time (MRT).
4. **Critical Flicker Fusion (CFF):** Evaluates central integrative capacity. Subjects observe flickering lights until they fuse into a continuous beam (measured in Hertz). BZDs significantly decrease the CFF threshold.
5. **Subjective Rating Scales:** Visual Analog Scales (VAS) evaluating calmness vs. agitation, tiredness vs. dynamism.

*Note on Dosing:* While high clinical doses of BZDs impair performance, very low doses (e.g., lorazepam 0.25 mg or alprazolam 0.125 mg) can actually improve psychometric performance by causing disinhibition. 

### 17.3 Phase II and Phase III Studies

**Phase II (Dose-Finding):**
* The objective is to identify the effective dosage range.
* Usually involves a 5-arm study (three doses of the test drug, a placebo, and a reference drug) or a 2-arm study against placebo. 
* A major difficulty is the high placebo response rate in GAD/ADAM (often around 50% after one month), making it statistically difficult to prove a minimal effective dose that is significantly superior to placebo.

**Phase III (Efficacy Confirmation):**
* Conducted on a larger scale (100–250 patients per group) to confirm efficacy against established reference drugs (like SSRIs or SNRIs).
* Because GAD is chronic, long-term evaluation (maintenance) is crucial. Patients treated successfully for 8 weeks are often randomized into a continuation group vs. a placebo withdrawal group to assess relapse rates.

### 17.4 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria
Clinical trials for GAD/ADAM must strictly define the patient population to avoid confounding variables.

**Inclusion Criteria:**
* Outpatients (GAD/ADAM rarely requires hospitalization).
* Age 18 to 65.
* **HAM-A Score:** Must be strictly greater than 20 and remain stable (not fluctuating more than 10%) during the placebo run-in/wash-out period.
* **Covi Scale vs. Raskin Scale:** The patient's score on the Covi anxiety scale must be greater than their score on the Raskin depression scale to ensure the primary pathology is anxiety, not depression.

**Exclusion Criteria:**
* Major depressive episode (e.g., a score > 15 on the Montgomery-Asberg Depression Rating Scale, MADRS).
* Any other DSM-IV anxiety disorder (e.g., Panic Disorder, OCD).
* Major weight problems (anorexia/bulimia), alcoholism, or major organic disease.
* Co-administration of any psychotropic or sedative drugs (including over-the-counter nasal vasoconstrictors) in the prior two weeks.

### 17.5 Evaluation Scales

**Primary and Secondary Scales:**
* **Hamilton Anxiety Scale (HAM-A):** The primary criterion. A 14-item scale, though only three items (anxious mood, attention, and behavior) strictly relate to GAD, with the rest being non-specific somatic symptoms.
* **Covi Scale:** Used for inclusion. A 3-item global evaluation assessing speech, behavior, and somatic complaints (scored 0-12). A score > 6 indicates clinical anxiety; a score < 3 indicates treatment success.
* **Tyrer Scale:** 10 items derived from the Comprehensive Psychiatric Rating Scale (CPRS), often used as a secondary measure.
* **Clinical Global Impression (CGI):** A 7-point scale. A score > 4 is an inclusion criterion, and a 2-point reduction indicates therapeutic success.

**Self-Rating Scales:**
* **Sheehan Incapacitation Scale:** A visual analogue scale where the patient grades how anxiety impairs three areas: work, family life, and social life. Reduces the variability of external assessor biases.

### 17.6 Discontinuation, Dependence, and Withdrawal
A major element of anxiolytic testing is assessing the drug's withdrawal profile. BZD dependence is a physiological adaptation, not necessarily an addiction. 
* **Recurrence:** Return of the original symptoms at the original intensity, occurring slowly over weeks/months after stopping.
* **Rebound:** Symptoms returning *more intensely* than baseline, usually within hours or days of discontinuation.
* **Withdrawal:** Novel physiological symptoms (different from the original anxiety) caused by declining blood levels of the drug. 
* *Risk Factors for Severe Withdrawal:* High daily dose, duration > 6 months, short half-life drugs, and abrupt discontinuation.

**Withdrawal Assessment Tools:**
To accurately capture these phenomena, trials utilize both physician and patient-rated scales alongside standard EEG and endocrinological evaluations (like plasma cortisol).
* *Physician-Rated:* Tranquilizer Withdrawal Scale (11 items) and the Benzodiazepine Withdrawal Check-list (16 items).
* *Patient-Rated:* Benzodiazepine Withdrawal Symptom Questionnaire (20 items) and the Withdrawal Symptom Score (14 items).

## 18. A Case Study of the 35% $CO_2$ Challenge
**Authors:** K. Verburg, G. Perna and E.J.L. Griez

### 18.1 Introduction to the 35% $CO_2$ Challenge
The 35% $CO_2$ challenge has become a highly reliable and specific experimental methodology for provoking panic attacks in clinical settings. It serves as a vital biological marker for Panic Disorder (PD), differentiating it from other anxiety disorders and healthy controls. 

### 18.2 Clinical Protocol and Safety Parameters
Because experimental psychiatry involves inducing severe discomfort (panic), strict protocols and ethical safety parameters are mandatory. The procedure must be fully reversible, under complete physician control, and cause no long-term psychological or physical damage.

**Inclusion Criteria:**
* **Patients:** Must meet DSM-IV criteria for PD, confirmed by at least two experienced clinicians or via structured interview.
* **Controls:** Must be in good physical health with an absolute absence of any present or past psychiatric illness.

**Absolute Exclusion Criteria:**
* **Cardiovascular:** History or suspicion of infarct, cardiomyopathy, cardiac failure, Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA), angina pectoris, cardiac arrhythmias, or Cerebrovascular Accident (CVA).
* **Respiratory:** Asthma and lung fibrosis.
* **Neurological/Other:** Personal or familial history of cerebral aneurysm, severe hypertension (systolic > 180, diastolic > 100 mmHg), and pregnancy.
* **Relative Contraindications:** Age < 15 or > 60 years, epilepsy, and non-invalidating Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD).

**Pre-Challenge Restrictions:**
* Two weeks free of central-acting medications (including Beta-Blockers). An exception is made for incidental use of very low-dose benzodiazepines (equivalent to max 5 mg diazepam).
* 36 hours without excessive alcohol consumption.
* 8 hours without xanthine-containing beverages (caffeine).
* 2 hours of strict fasting, no smoking, and no xanthine.

### 18.3 The Inhalation Procedure (Maastricht Academic Anxiety Center)
1. **Vital Capacity:** The patient's vital capacity is measured.
2. **Double-Blind Administration:** Two gas mixtures (air-placebo vs. 35% $CO_2$/65% $O_2$) are administered in randomised order.
3. **Instruction:** Patients are informed they will inhale harmless physiological compounds that may cause sensations ranging from mild to "frank anxiety." (The term "panic attack" is strictly avoided to prevent priming).
4. **Execution:** The subject takes the mask, exhales deeply, presses the mask to their face, and takes a vital capacity inhalation (the experimenter ensures a minimum of 80% vital capacity is reached). 
5. **Retention:** The subject holds their breath while the experimenter counts aloud for exactly 4 seconds before exhalation.
6. **Wash-out:** A 15-minute rest period occurs between the two gas mixtures.

### 18.4 Measurement and Psychometric Evaluation
Subjects are evaluated immediately before and after the inhalation using specific scales:
* **Visual Anxiety Analogue Scale (VAAS):** A scale ranging from 0 (no anxiety) to 100 (the worst anxiety imaginable).
* **DSM-IV Symptom List:** Evaluates the 13 core symptoms of a panic attack, each scored from 0 (not at all) to 4 (very intensive), yielding a maximum score of 52.

### 18.5 Data Analysis and the "Ceiling Effect"
A significant methodological challenge in quantifying the panic response is dealing with baseline anxiety (the "ceiling effect"). If a patient starts with a baseline VAAS of 90 and goes to 100, is the Delta score (+10) equivalent to a patient going from 0 to 10? 
* **Percentage Score (%VAS-A):** To overcome this, researchers calculate the percentage of the maximum possible increment or decrement.
    * If anxiety increases: `%VAS-A = (Delta VAS-A * 100) / (100 - Baseline VAS-A)`
    * If anxiety decreases: `%VAS-A = (Delta VAS-A * 100) / Baseline VAS-A`
* **ROC Analysis:** Despite these mathematical corrections, Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) analysis suggests that simply using the **absolute VAS score after $CO_2$ inhalation** might actually be the most valid measure for distinguishing true PD patients from patients with other anxiety disorders, as it avoids the mathematical paradoxes generated when baseline anxiety is extremely high or zero.

## 19. The Tryptophan Depletion Technique
**Authors:** (Standard Clinical Synthesis based on the compendium text)

### 19.1 Introduction to Tryptophan Depletion (TD)
The Tryptophan Depletion (TD) technique—sometimes referred to as Acute Tryptophan Depletion (ATD)—is a highly specific experimental method used to temporarily and safely lower central serotonin (5-HT) levels in the human brain. It serves as a crucial tool for investigating the role of the serotonergic system in the pathophysiology and treatment of anxiety and affective disorders.

### 19.2 The Mechanism of 5-HT Synthesis and Depletion
To understand the TD technique, one must understand how the brain synthesizes serotonin.
* **The Precursor:** Serotonin cannot cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB). The brain must synthesize it locally from the essential amino acid **tryptophan (TRP)**, which is obtained through diet.
* **The Transport Mechanism:** TRP crosses the BBB via an active transport mechanism that it shares with other Large Neutral Amino Acids (LNAAs), such as valine, leucine, isoleucine, tyrosine, and phenylalanine. TRP competes with these LNAAs for entry into the brain.
* **The Depletion Protocol:** The subject ingests a specific, highly concentrated amino acid mixture that contains abundant LNAAs but is **completely devoid of TRP**. 
* **The Dual Effect:** 1. The massive influx of amino acids stimulates protein synthesis in the liver, which pulls available circulating TRP out of the blood plasma.
    2. The overwhelming ratio of competing LNAAs to TRP in the bloodstream completely monopolizes the BBB transport system, effectively blocking any remaining TRP from entering the brain.
* **Result:** Within 5 to 7 hours of ingestion, central 5-HT levels drop drastically (by up to 80-90% as measured by CSF metabolites), allowing researchers a temporary window to study the effects of acute serotonergic deficiency.

### 19.3 Effects on Healthy Volunteers
A critical baseline finding in TD research is its effect on individuals with no psychiatric history.
* **Mood and Anxiety:** In strictly healthy volunteers (those lacking a personal or family history of psychiatric illness), massive depletion of central serotonin **does not** induce clinical depression or anxiety disorders. 
* **Sub-Clinical Effects:** It may cause mild irritability, slightly lowered mood, or alterations in sleep architecture, but it fails to provoke panic attacks or obsessions. This proves that low serotonin alone is not the sole cause of anxiety or depression; a pre-existing neurobiological or genetic vulnerability is required.

### 19.4 Effects in Anxiety Disorders
The primary utility of the TD technique has been unmasking serotonergic vulnerabilities in clinical populations.

**Panic Disorder (PD):**
* **Baseline Provocation:** Unlike the 35% $CO_2$ challenge or sodium lactate, administering the TD drink to untreated PD patients does *not* reliably provoke spontaneous panic attacks. 
* **Exacerbation of Vulnerability:** However, TD significantly worsens the panic response if the patient is subsequently exposed to a $CO_2$ challenge. 
* **Treatment Reversal:** In PD patients who have been successfully treated with SSRIs (and are currently asymptomatic), the TD technique causes a transient, rapid return of panic symptoms. This strongly suggests that SSRIs maintain their antipanic efficacy by ensuring a continuous, enhanced availability of 5-HT at the synapse.

**Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD):**
* **Baseline Effects:** Administering TD to untreated OCD patients does not generally exacerbate their baseline obsessions or compulsions. 
* **Treatment Reversal:** Similar to PD, in OCD patients who are in remission due to SSRI treatment, acute tryptophan depletion causes a transient relapse of obsessive-compulsive symptoms. The symptoms peak at the height of depletion (approx. 5-7 hours) and resolve within 24 hours as normal diet resumes and 5-HT levels restore.

**Social Phobia and PTSD:**
* While less extensively studied with this specific paradigm compared to PD and OCD, emerging data suggests that TD can unmask vulnerabilities to social scrutiny and increase baseline hyperarousal, further validating the broad role of serotonin in modulating threat-detection across the anxiety spectrum.

### 19.5 Methodological Considerations and Limitations
* **The "Sham" Control:** To ensure blinding and control for placebo effects, subjects must undergo a "sham depletion" on a separate day. This involves ingesting the exact same foul-tasting amino acid mixture, but with a normal physiological dose of tryptophan added back in. 
* **Side Effects:** The primary limitation of the TD technique is the unpalatability of the amino acid mixture. Nausea and vomiting are frequent side effects. Because nausea can independently cause distress and physiological arousal, researchers must carefully control for it so that drug-induced sickness is not falsely recorded as a panic attack or anxiety spike.
