# Chapter 1: Introducing Psychology
**Psychology** is the scientific study of mind and behavior. The field explores a vast array of topics from biological influences on behavior to social and cultural effects.

### 1.1 Psychology as a Science
* **Intuition vs. Science:** Relying on "common sense" or intuition is insufficient for understanding behavior. People often accept explanations that seem "right" without thorough testing (e.g., eyewitness misidentification).
* **Unconscious Biases:** Humans are often unaware of the causes of their own behavior. Research shows people may unconsciously prefer brands containing letters of their own name.
* **Hindsight Bias:** The tendency to believe one could have predicted an outcome after it has already occurred.
* **Empirical Methods:** Psychologists rely on the **scientific method**—a set of rules and procedures to collect, analyze, and interpret data objectively.
* **Values vs. Facts:** * *Values:* Personal statements (e.g., "Abortion should be legal"). Science cannot prove/disprove values.
    * *Facts:* Objective statements determined via empirical study (e.g., "Homicide rates in 2009").

### 1.2 Levels of Explanation in Psychology
Psychological phenomena are **multiply determined** (caused by many factors) and must be understood at different levels:
1.  **Lower Level (Biological):** Focuses on genes, neurons, neurotransmitters, and hormones (e.g., genetic predisposition to depression).
2.  **Middle Level (Interpersonal/Individual):** Focuses on individual abilities, characteristics, and personal experiences (e.g., negative thought patterns).
3.  **Higher Level (Social/Cultural):** Focuses on groups, organizations, and cultural influences (e.g., cultural differences in how depression is expressed).

### 1.3 The Evolution of Psychology: History and Schools of Thought
Psychology evolved from philosophy into a scientific discipline. Major schools of thought include:

| School | Focus | Key Figures |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Structuralism** | Identifying basic elements ("structures") of psychological experience through introspection. | Wilhelm Wundt, Edward B. Titchener |
| **Functionalism** | Understanding *why* humans/animals have developed particular psychological aspects (evolutionary purpose). | William James |
| **Psychodynamic** | Role of unconscious thoughts, feelings, memories, and early childhood experiences. | Sigmund Freud, Carl Jung, Alfred Adler, Erik Erikson |
| **Behaviorism** | Premise that the mind cannot be studied objectively; focus strictly on observable behavior. | John B. Watson, B. F. Skinner |
| **Cognitive** | Study of mental processes: perception, thinking, memory, and judgment. | Hermann Ebbinghaus, Jean Piaget |
| **Social-Cultural** | How social situations and cultures influence thinking and behavior. | Fritz Heider, Leon Festinger, Stanley Schachter |

### 1.4 Important Questions in Psychology
The discipline grapples with several foundational debates:
* **Nature vs. Nurture:** Are genes or environment more influential in determining behavior? (Most modern psychologists agree both interact).
* **Free Will vs. Determinism:** Are we the authors of our actions, or is behavior determined by factors outside our control?
* **Accuracy vs. Inaccuracy:** To what extent are humans efficient information processors vs. prone to cognitive biases?
* **Conscious vs. Unconscious Processing:** How much of our behavior is guided by factors we are aware of?
* **Differences vs. Similarities:** To what extent are people similar, and to what extent are we different across genders, ethnicities, and cultures?

### 1.5 Careers in Psychology
Psychology offers diverse paths, including:
* **Clinical/Counseling:** Assessing and treating mental disorders.
* **Industrial-Organizational (I/O):** Applying principles to the workplace.
* **Forensic:** Applying psychology to the legal system.
* **School:** Working with students, teachers, and parents.
* **Research:** Conducting basic or applied studies in labs.

# Chapter 2: Psychological Science
Psychology relies on the scientific method to differentiate fact from value. This chapter outlines the rigorous procedures used to study human behavior.

### 2.1 The Scientific Method: Organizing Principles
* **Laws:** Principles so general they apply to all situations in a given domain (e.g., Law of Effect).
* **Theories:** Integrated sets of principles that explain and predict many observed relationships. Good theories are:
  * *General:* Summarize many different outcomes.
  * *Parsimonious:* Provide the simplest possible explanation.
  * *Falsifiable:* Relationship among variables can be shown through research to be incorrect.
* **Research Hypothesis:** A specific and falsifiable prediction regarding the relationship between two or more variables.
* **Operational Definitions:** Transforming conceptual variables (e.g., aggression, depression) into measured variables (e.g., number of presses on a button, score on a test).

### 2.2 Ethical Research
* **Human Research:** Psychologists must balance the potential benefit of research against the potential cost to participants.
  * *Informed Consent:* Participants must be told the nature of the research, their right to withdraw, and the potential risks.
  * *Deception:* Only used if justified by the study's scientific value and when alternative procedures are not feasible.
  * *Debriefing:* Full explanation of the study's purpose after participation.
* **Animal Research:** Essential for understanding basic processes; researchers must ensure humane treatment and minimize pain.

### 2.3 Research Designs
| Design | Goal | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Descriptive** | Create a snapshot of the current state of affairs. | Complete picture of behavior; allows for question development. | Does not assess relationships among variables. |
| **Correlational** | Assess the relationship between two or more variables. | Allows testing of expected relationships/predictions. | Cannot be used to draw causal conclusions. |
| **Experimental** | Assess the causal impact of one or more manipulations. | Allows for causal conclusions. | Cannot experimentally manipulate many important variables (e.g., poverty). |

* **Experimental Variables:**
  * *Independent Variable:* The variable manipulated by the researcher.
  * *Dependent Variable:* The measured variable expected to change based on the manipulation.
  * *Random Assignment:* Ensures any differences between groups are due to chance, maximizing internal validity.

### 2.4 Threats to Validity
Research must be valid to be useful. There are four primary threats:
1.  **Construct Validity:** Extent to which measured variables adequately assess the conceptual variables. Requires *Reliability* (consistency).
2.  **Statistical Conclusion Validity:** Extent to which the researcher has drawn accurate conclusions about the statistical significance (p < 0.05).
3.  **Internal Validity:** Extent to which we can trust the causal relationship conclusion. Maximized by eliminating **Confounding Variables** (variables other than the independent variable on which participants differ systematically).
4.  **External Validity:** Extent to which results can be generalized across different groups, settings, or times. Replication is the primary defense against external validity threats.

### 2.5 Common Biases in Research
* **Experimenter Bias:** Researchers' expectations influence the results. Prevented using **Double-Blind Studies** where neither the participant nor the experimenter knows the condition.
* **Placebo Effects (Participant Bias):** Participants' expectations influence their behavior.

# Chapter 3: Brains, Bodies, and Behavior
This chapter explores the biological foundations of psychology, detailing how the brain, nervous system, and endocrine system interact to produce behavior and mental processes.

### 3.1 The Neuron: The Building Block of the Nervous System
* **Neuron:** A cell in the nervous system whose function it is to receive and transmit information.


[Image of the structure of a motor neuron]

  * **Soma (Cell Body):** Contains the nucleus and keeps the cell alive.
  * **Dendrites:** Branch-like structures that receive information from other neurons.
  * **Axon:** Transmits information away from the cell body toward other neurons or muscles.
  * **Myelin Sheath:** A layer of fatty tissue that insulates the axon and speeds up the transmission of impulses.
* **Communication Process:**
  * *Action Potential:* An electrical charge that travels down the axon.
  * *Synapse:* The small gap between neurons where information is transmitted chemically.
  * *Neurotransmitters:* Chemical messengers released into the synapse to bind with receptor sites on the receiving neuron.

### 3.2 Major Neurotransmitters and Their Functions
| Neurotransmitter | Primary Functions | Associated Disorders |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Serotonin** | Sleep, appetite, mood regulation. | Low levels linked to depression. |
| **Dopamine** | Movement, motivation, reward/pleasure. | High levels linked to Schizophrenia; low levels to Parkinson's. |
| **Acetylcholine (ACh)** | Muscle movement, memory, and learning. | Undersupply linked to Alzheimer’s. |
| **GABA** | Major inhibitory neurotransmitter; slows down CNS. | Low levels linked to anxiety and tremors. |
| **Glutamate** | Major excitatory neurotransmitter; memory. | Excess can cause seizures or migraines. |
| **Endorphins** | Natural pain relievers; released during exercise. | Related to "runner's high." |

### 3.3 The Brain: Structure and Function


[Image of the major structures in the human brain]

The brain is categorized into three major layers based on evolutionary development:
1.  **The Brainstem (The "Old Brain"):** Controls basic survival functions like breathing, heart rate, and sleep (includes the Medulla, Pons, and Reticular Formation).
2.  **The Limbic System:** Located between the brainstem and the cerebral hemispheres; associated with emotions and drives.
    * *Amygdala:* Regulates fear and aggression.
    * *Hippocampus:* Essential for forming long-term memories.
    * *Hypothalamus:* Links the nervous system to the endocrine system; regulates hunger, thirst, and body temperature.
3.  **The Cerebral Cortex:** The outer layer of the brain responsible for high-level processes like language, complex thought, and consciousness.
    * *Frontal Lobe:* Thinking, planning, and judgment (contains the Motor Cortex).
    * *Parietal Lobe:* Processes touch and body position (contains the Sensory Cortex).
    * *Temporal Lobe:* Hearing and language.
    * *Occipital Lobe:* Visual processing.

### 3.4 The Nervous and Endocrine Systems
* **Central Nervous System (CNS):** The brain and spinal cord.
* **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** Links the CNS to the body’s sense receptors, muscles, and glands.
  * *Somatic:* Controls skeletal muscles.
  * *Autonomic:* Regulates internal organs (e.g., heart, lungs).
    * **Sympathetic:** "Fight or Flight" (arouses the body).
    * **Parasympathetic:** "Rest and Digest" (calms the body).


[Image of the autonomic nervous system]

* **Endocrine System:** A chemical communication system using **Hormones** secreted by glands into the bloodstream.
  * *Pituitary Gland:* The "Master Gland"; controls growth and other glands.
  * *Adrenal Glands:* Secrete epinephrine and norepinephrine during stress.

### 3.5 Neuroplasticity and the Flexible Brain
* **Neuroplasticity:** The brain's ability to change its structure and function in response to experience or damage. This is the biological basis for all learning and rehabilitation.
* **Neurogenesis:** The formation of new neurons, which continues into adulthood, particularly in the hippocampus.

# Chapter 4: Sensing and Perceiving
This chapter explores how we receive information from the environment through our senses (**sensation**) and how our brains organize and interpret that information (**perception**).

### 4.1 Sensation and Sensory Thresholds
* **Sensation:** The process by which our sensory receptors and nervous system receive and represent stimulus energies from our environment.
* **Perception:** The process of organizing and interpreting sensory information, enabling us to recognize meaningful objects and events.
* **Transduction:** The conversion of one form of energy into another. In sensation, it's the transformation of stimulus energies (sights, sounds, smells) into neural impulses.
* **Sensory Thresholds:**
    * *Absolute Threshold:* The minimum stimulation needed to detect a particular stimulus (light, sound, pressure, taste, or odor) 50% of the time.
    * *Difference Threshold (Just Noticeable Difference - JND):* The minimum difference between two stimuli required for detection 50% of the time.
    * *Weber’s Law:* To be perceived as different, two stimuli must differ by a constant minimum percentage (rather than a constant amount).
* **Signal Detection Analysis:** A technique used to determine the ability of the perceiver to separate true signals from background noise. It accounts for both sensitivity and response bias.

### 4.2 Seeing
* **The Visual System:**
    * Light enters the eye through the *cornea*, passes through the *pupil* (controlled by the *iris*), and is focused by the *lens* onto the *retina*.
    [Image of anatomy of the human eye]
    * **Retina Cells:**
        * *Rods:* Detect black, white, and gray; necessary for peripheral and twilight vision.
        * *Cones:* Detect fine detail and color; function in daylight or well-lit conditions.
* **Visual Pathways:** Information travels from the retina via the *optic nerve* to the *thalamus* and then to the *visual cortex* in the occipital lobe.
* **Color Vision:**
    * *Young-Helmholtz Trichromatic Theory:* The retina contains three types of color receptors (red, green, blue).
    * *Opponent-Process Theory:* Opposing retinal processes (red-green, yellow-blue, white-black) enable color vision.

### 4.3 Gestalt Principles of Form Perception
We don't just see patches of color; we see organized "wholes."
* **Figure and Ground:** Structuring the visual field into objects (the figures) that stand out from their surroundings (the ground).
* **Grouping Principles:**
    * *Similarity:* Grouping similar figures together.
    * *Proximity:* Grouping nearby figures together.
    * *Continuity:* Perceiving smooth, continuous patterns rather than discontinuous ones.
    * *Closure:* Filling in gaps to create a complete, whole object.
    [Image of gestalt principles of perception]

### 4.4 Hearing, Tasting, Smelling, and Touching
* **Hearing:** Sound waves are collected by the *outer ear*, amplified by the *middle ear* (ossicles), and converted into neural impulses in the *inner ear* (cochlea).
    [Image of anatomy of the human ear]
* **Tasting (Gustation):** Five basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami.
* **Smelling (Olfaction):** Chemical molecules enter the nose and hit receptor cells at the top of the nasal cavity. Olfactory information bypasses the thalamus and goes straight to the olfactory bulb.
* **Touching (Somatosensation):** Includes pressure, warmth, cold, and pain.
    * *Gate-Control Theory of Pain:* The spinal cord contains a neurological "gate" that either blocks pain signals or allows them to pass on to the brain.
* **Proprioception and Vestibular System:**
    * *Proprioception:* Sensing the position and movement of individual body parts.
    * *Vestibular System:* Sensing body orientation and balance (located in the inner ear).

### 4.5 Accuracy and Inaccuracy in Perception
Perception is an active, constructive process influenced by expectations and context.
* **Selective Attention:** The focusing of conscious awareness on a particular stimulus (e.g., the cocktail party effect).
* **Perceptual Constancy:** Perceiving objects as unchanging (having consistent color, brightness, shape, and size) even as illumination and retinal images change.
* **Illusions:** Demonstrations of how the brain can be misled by environmental cues.
    * *Mueller-Lyre Illusion:* Misperceiving the length of lines based on arrowheads.
    * *Ponzo Illusion:* Linear perspective cues lead to misperceiving size.
* **The Importance of Expectations:** Our *perceptual set* (a mental predisposition to perceive one thing and not another) heavily influences what we see and hear.

# Chapter 5: States of Consciousness
This chapter examines the nature of consciousness, the physiological stages of sleep, the purpose of dreams, and how psychoactive drugs alter brain chemistry and behavior.

### 5.1 The Nature of Consciousness
* **Consciousness:** Our subjective awareness of ourselves and our environment. It is functional, allowing us to plan, monitor our goals, and navigate social environments.
* **Biological Rhythms:**
    * *Circadian Rhythm:* The internal 24-hour biological clock that guides the sleep-wake cycle. It is influenced by exposure to light, which triggers the pineal gland to release or suppress **melatonin**.

### 5.2 Sleep Stages and the Sleeping Brain
Sleep is not a "passive" state but a highly structured physiological process consisting of distinct stages.* **NREM Stage 1 (N1):** The transition between wakefulness and sleep; characterized by theta waves and sudden twitches (hypnagogic jerks).
* **NREM Stage 2 (N2):** Characterized by "sleep spindles" (bursts of rapid brain activity). This makes up about 50% of total sleep time.
* **NREM Stage 3 (N3):** Deep sleep or "slow-wave sleep," characterized by delta waves. This is the stage where the body repairs itself and growth hormones are released.
* **REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep:** Characterized by fast, random brain waves (similar to wakefulness), rapid eye movements, and muscle paralysis. This is the stage where most vivid dreaming occurs.


### 5.3 Sleep Disorders and the Costs of Deprivation
* **Common Disorders:**
    * *Insomnia:* Persistent difficulty falling or staying asleep.
    * *Sleep Apnea:* Repeatedly stopping breathing during sleep, leading to frequent awakenings.
    * *Narcolepsy:* Extreme daytime sleepiness with sudden "sleep attacks" into REM sleep.
* **Sleep Deprivation:** Leads to increased cortisol (stress), suppressed immune function, obesity, and impaired cognitive performance (similar to alcohol intoxication).

### 5.4 Dreams and Dreaming
* **Freud’s Psychoanalytic Theory:** Dreams represent unconscious wishes and conflicts.
    * *Manifest Content:* The literal storyline of the dream.
    * *Latent Content:* The hidden psychological meaning.
* **Activation-Synthesis Theory:** Dreams are the brain’s attempt to make sense of random neural firing in the brainstem during REM sleep.

### 5.5 Psychoactive Drugs
Drugs that alter consciousness by affecting the biochemical operations of the brain at the synapse.

# Chapter 5: States of Consciousness
This chapter explores the nature of **consciousness**—our subjective awareness of ourselves and our environment—and how it is altered through sleep, drugs, and psychological techniques.

### 5.1 Sleeping and Dreaming
* **Consciousness:** Our subjective awareness of internal and external stimuli.
* **Circadian Rhythm:** Internal biological clock; regular bodily rhythms (e.g., temperature and wakefulness) that occur on a 24-hour cycle.
* **Stages of Sleep:** We move through four distinct stages every 90 minutes.
    * *Stage N1 (Non-REM):* Light sleep; characterized by theta waves and sudden jerks (hypnagogic sensations).
    * *Stage N2 (Non-REM):* Characterized by "sleep spindles" (bursts of rapid, rhythmic brain-wave activity).
    * *Stage N3 (Non-REM):* Deep sleep; characterized by large, slow delta waves. This is when sleepwalking and night terrors usually occur.
    * *REM Sleep (Rapid Eye Movement):* Paradoxical sleep; brain activity resembles wakefulness, but muscles are paralyzed. This is the stage where vivid dreaming occurs.
    [Image of sleep stages and brain activity]
* **Sleep Disorders:**
    * *Insomnia:* Persistent problems in falling or staying asleep.
    * *Narcolepsy:* Sudden, uncontrollable sleep attacks.
    * *Sleep Apnea:* Temporary cessations of breathing during sleep and repeated momentary awakenings.
* **Why We Dream:**
    * *Freud's Theory:* Dreams provide a "safety valve" to express otherwise unacceptable feelings (Manifest vs. Latent content).
    * *Activation-Synthesis Theory:* Dreams are the brain’s attempt to make sense of random neural activity originating in the brainstem.

### 5.2 Altering Consciousness with Psychoactive Drugs
* **Psychoactive Drugs:** Chemical substances that alter perceptions and moods.
* **Key Concepts:**
    * *Tolerance:* Diminishing effect with regular use of the same dose.
    * *Withdrawal:* Discomfort and distress following discontinuing an addictive drug.
    * *Physical Dependence:* Physiological need for a drug (marked by withdrawal).
    * *Psychological Dependence:* Emotional need to use a drug (to relieve negative emotions).

#### Classes of Psychoactive Drugs
| Class | Mechanism | Symptoms | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Stimulants** | Block reuptake of dopamine/norepinephrine. | Increased heart rate, energy, mood. | Caffeine, Nicotine, Cocaine, Amphetamines. |
| **Depressants** | Increase production of GABA; decrease Glutamate. | Calming effect, slowed reaction, reduced anxiety. | Alcohol, Barbiturates, Benzodiazepines. |
| **Opioids** | Mimic endorphins; bind to opioid receptors. | Pain relief, euphoria, sleepiness. | Opium, Morphine, Heroin, Codeine. |
| **Hallucinogens** | Mimic serotonin/epinephrine. | Altered perception, hallucinations. | Marijuana, LSD, Mescaline, PCP. |

### 5.3 Other Altered States: Hypnosis, Sensory Deprivation, and Meditation
* **Hypnosis:** A social interaction in which one person suggests to another that certain perceptions, feelings, thoughts, or behaviors will spontaneously occur.
    * *Clinical Use:* Effective for pain management (hypnotic analgesia) and reducing anxiety.
* **Sensory Deprivation:** Intentionally reducing sensory input to alter consciousness. While potentially relaxing, prolonged deprivation can lead to hallucinations.
* **Meditation:** A set of techniques used to train attention and awareness, and achieve a mentally clear and emotionally calm and stable state.
    * *Benefits:* Reduces stress, lowers blood pressure, and improves markers of cardiovascular health.

# Chapter 6: Growing and Developing
This chapter examines **developmental psychology**—the study of the physical, cognitive, and social changes that occur throughout the human lifespan, from conception to death.

### 6.1 Prenatal Development and the Newborn
* **Stages of Prenatal Development:**
    1.  **Zygote:** Conception to 2 weeks. Rapid cell division.
    2.  **Embryo:** 2 weeks to 8 weeks. Major internal and external organs begin to form.
    3.  **Fetus:** 9 weeks to birth. Growth and finishing touches.
* **Teratogens:** Environmental agents (e.g., alcohol, drugs, viruses) that can damage the developing fetus.
* **Newborn Reflexes:** Built-in physical responses for survival, such as the *Rooting Reflex* (turning toward a touch on the cheek to find food) and the *Moro Reflex* (extending limbs when startled).

### 6.2 Cognitive Development in Childhood (Jean Piaget)
Piaget argued that children are not "little adults" but think in fundamentally different ways.
* **Key Processes:**
    * *Schemas:* Mental frameworks that help interpret information.
    * *Assimilation:* Fitting new info into existing schemas.
    * *Accommodation:* Modifying schemas to fit new info.
* **Piaget’s Four Stages:**
    1.  **Sensorimotor (Birth–2 yrs):** Learning through senses and motor actions. Key milestone: *Object Permanence* (knowing things exist even when out of sight).
    2.  **Preoperational (2–7 yrs):** Use of symbols/language but lack logical reasoning. Characterized by *Egocentrism* (inability to see others' perspectives) and a lack of *Conservation*.
    3.  **Concrete Operational (7–11 yrs):** Ability to think logically about concrete events and master conservation.
    4.  **Formal Operational (12+ yrs):** Ability to think abstractly and use hypothetical reasoning.### 6.3 Social Development: Attachment and Self-Concept
* **Attachment:** The emotional bond between infant and caregiver. 
    * *Harlow’s Research:* Demonstrated that "contact comfort" (touch/warmth) is more important than food in creating attachment.
    * *Ainsworth’s Strange Situation:* Identified three styles:
        * **Secure Attachment:** Distressed when parent leaves, happy on return.
        * **Insecure-Avoidant:** Indifferent to parent's presence or absence.
        * **Insecure-Resistant:** Clingy but angry/upset upon parent's return.
* **Self-Concept:** By 18 months, infants recognize themselves in a mirror. By school age, they describe themselves in terms of traits and group memberships.

### 6.4 Adolescence: Physical, Cognitive, and Moral Shifts
* **Puberty:** Period of rapid physical growth and sexual maturation.
* **Moral Development (Lawrence Kohlberg):**
    1.  **Preconventional:** Moral reasoning based on self-interest (reward/punishment).
    2.  **Conventional:** Based on social rules and laws.
    3.  **Postconventional:** Based on self-defined ethical principles.
* **Identity Formation (James Marcia):** Adolescents go through stages of *Diffusion* (no commitment), *Foreclosure* (premature commitment), *Moratorium* (active searching), and *Achievement*.

### 6.5 Adulthood and Aging
* **Erikson’s Psychosocial Stages (Adulthood):**
    * *Early Adulthood:* Intimacy vs. Isolation.
    * *Middle Adulthood:* Generativity vs. Stagnation (contributing to the next generation).
    * *Late Adulthood:* Ego Integrity vs. Despair (reflecting on life).


[Image of Erikson's stages of psychosocial development]

* **Parenting Styles (Baumrind):**
    * *Authoritative:* High demandingness, high responsiveness (Best outcomes).
    * *Authoritarian:* High demandingness, low responsiveness.
    * *Permissive:* Low demandingness, high responsiveness.
    * *Neglectful:* Low demandingness, low responsiveness.
* **Cognitive Changes in Aging:** *Fluid Intelligence* (speed of processing) declines, but *Crystallized Intelligence* (knowledge and vocabulary) remains stable or increases.
* **Death and Dying:** Elizabeth Kübler-Ross identified five stages of grief: Denial, Anger, Bargaining, Depression, and Acceptance.

# Chapter 7: Learning
This chapter explores **learning**—a relatively permanent change in behavior or knowledge that results from experience. It covers the three major types of learning: classical conditioning, operant conditioning, and observational learning.

### 7.1 Classical Conditioning (Pavlovian Conditioning)
* **Classical Conditioning:** A type of learning in which one learns to link two or more stimuli and anticipate events.
* **The Process:**
    * **Unconditioned Stimulus (US):** A stimulus that naturally and automatically triggers a response (e.g., food).
    * **Unconditioned Response (UR):** The unlearned, naturally occurring response to the US (e.g., salivation).
    * **Conditioned Stimulus (CS):** An originally neutral stimulus that, after association with an US, comes to trigger a conditioned response (e.g., a bell).
    * **Conditioned Response (CR):** The learned response to a previously neutral CS (e.g., salivation to the bell).
[Image of classical conditioning process]
* **Key Principles:**
    * *Acquisition:* The initial stage of learning.
    * *Extinction:* The diminishing of a CR when the US no longer follows the CS.
    * *Spontaneous Recovery:* The reappearance, after a pause, of an extinguished CR.
    * *Generalization:* The tendency for stimuli similar to the CS to elicit similar responses.
    * *Discrimination:* The learned ability to distinguish between a CS and other irrelevant stimuli.

### 7.2 Operant Conditioning
* **Operant Conditioning:** A type of learning in which behavior is strengthened if followed by a reinforcer or diminished if followed by a punisher.
* **Law of Effect (Thorndike):** Behaviors followed by favorable consequences become more likely, and behaviors followed by unfavorable consequences become less likely.
* **Reinforcement vs. Punishment:**
    * **Positive Reinforcement:** Adding a desirable stimulus to increase behavior (e.g., a reward).
    * **Negative Reinforcement:** Removing an aversive stimulus to increase behavior (e.g., taking an aspirin to stop pain).
    * **Positive Punishment:** Adding an aversive stimulus to decrease behavior (e.g., a speeding ticket).
    * **Negative Punishment:** Removing a desirable stimulus to decrease behavior (e.g., taking away phone privileges).
[Image of reinforcement and punishment matrix]

#### Schedules of Reinforcement
| Schedule | Description | Response Pattern |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Fixed-Ratio** | Reinforces a response only after a specified number of responses. | High rate of responding; brief pause after reinforcement. |
| **Variable-Ratio** | Reinforces a response after an unpredictable number of responses. | High, steady rate of responding; very resistant to extinction. |
| **Fixed-Interval** | Reinforces the first response after a fixed time period. | "Scalloped" response pattern; increases as time for reinforcement nears. |
| **Variable-Interval** | Reinforces the first response after unpredictable time intervals. | Slow, steady responding. |

### 7.3 Observational Learning
* **Observational Learning:** Learning by observing others (models).
* **Mirror Neurons:** Frontal lobe neurons that fire when performing certain actions or when observing another doing so.
* **Bandura’s Bobo Doll Experiment:** Demonstrated that children are highly likely to imitate the aggressive behavior of models they observe, especially if the models are rewarded.
* **Social Dilemmas:** Situations in which a self-interested choice by everyone creates the worst outcome for everyone (e.g., the *Prisoner’s Dilemma*).

### 7.4 Learning in Everyday Life
* **Advertising:** Uses classical conditioning to associate products with positive feelings.
* **Education:** Uses operant conditioning (rewards/punishment) to shape student behavior.
* **Undermining Intrinsic Interest:** Over-rewarding a task the person already enjoys can actually *decrease* their natural interest (the Overjustification Effect).

# Chapter 8: Remembering and Judging
This chapter focuses on **cognitive psychology**, specifically how we encode, store, and retrieve information (**memory**) and how we use that information to make decisions and judgments.

### 8.1 Memories as Types and Stages
Memory is not a single entity but a complex system categorized by type and stage.

#### Types of Memory
* **Explicit Memory (Declarative):** Knowledge or experiences that can be consciously remembered.
    * *Semantic Memory:* Knowledge of facts and concepts about the world (e.g., the capital of France).
    * *Episodic Memory:* First-hand experiences (e.g., your last birthday).
* **Implicit Memory (Non-declarative):** Influence of experience on behavior even if the individual is not aware of those influences.
    * *Procedural Memory:* Motor skills and habits (e.g., riding a bike).
    * *Classical Conditioning Effects:* Learned emotional responses.
    * *Priming:* Enhanced identification of objects or words.
[Image of types of memory flowchart]

#### Stages of Memory
1.  **Sensory Memory:** Brief storage of sensory information.
    * *Iconic Memory:* Visual sensory memory (lasts < 1 second).
    * *Echoic Memory:* Auditory sensory memory (lasts up to 4 seconds).
2.  **Short-Term Memory (STM):** Small amount of information is held for a short time (usually < 30 seconds).
    * *Working Memory:* The processes we use to make sense of, modify, and store information in STM.
    * *Capacity:* The "Magic Number 7 +/- 2." Information capacity can be increased via **Chunking**.
3.  **Long-Term Memory (LTM):** Memory storage that can hold information for days, months, and years.

### 8.2 Processes: Encoding, Storage, and Retrieval
* **Encoding:** The process by which we place the things that we experience into memory.
    * *Elaborative Encoding:* Processing new info in ways that make it more relevant or meaningful.
    * *Self-Reference Effect:* We remember information better when we relate it to ourselves.
* **Storage:** Maintaining information over time.
* **Retrieval:** The process of accessing stored information.
    * *Recognition vs. Recall:* Recognition (multiple-choice) is easier than recall (essay questions).
    * *Context-Dependent Learning:* Better retrieval when in the same environment where learning occurred.
    * *State-Dependent Learning:* Better retrieval when in the same psychological/physiological state (e.g., mood).
    [Image of the memory process: encoding, storage, retrieval]

### 8.3 Accuracy and Inaccuracy in Memory
Memory is **constructive**; we build memories based on what we expect or what makes sense.
* **The Forgetting Curve (Ebbinghaus):** Information is lost rapidly at first, then levels off.
* **Interference:**
    * *Proactive Interference:* Old info hinders recall of new info.
    * *Retroactive Interference:* New info hinders recall of old info.
* **Memory Errors:**
    * *Misinformation Effect:* Errors occur when new, misleading info is integrated into an existing memory (e.g., eyewitness testimony).
    * *Source Monitoring Error:* Uncertainty about the source of a memory (e.g., thinking a dream was a real event).
    * *Confirmation Bias:* Tendency to verify and confirm existing beliefs.

### 8.4 Heuristics and Judging
When making decisions, we often use mental shortcuts (**heuristics**) which can lead to errors.
* **Availability Heuristic:** Estimating the likelihood of events based on their availability in memory (e.g., fearing plane crashes more than car crashes because plane crashes are more salient in the news).
* **Representativeness Heuristic:** Judging the likelihood of things based on how well they match a particular prototype (stereotyping).
* **Cognitive Accessibility:** Some memories are more highly activated than others, leading us to overestimate our own contribution to group tasks.
* **Counterfactual Thinking:** Replaying events in our minds to imagine how they "could have" turned out differently (e.g., "if only I had left 5 minutes earlier").

# Chapter 9: Intelligence and Language
This chapter examines the nature of human **intelligence**—how we define, measure, and understand it—and **language**, the fundamental tool humans use for communication and thought.

### 9.1 Defining and Measuring Intelligence
* **Intelligence:** The ability to think, to learn from experience, to solve problems, and to adapt to new situations.
* **General Intelligence (g):** Proposed by Charles Spearman; the idea that there is a single, underlying factor that contributes to performance on all mental tasks.
* **Specific Intelligence (s):** A measure of specific skills in narrow domains.
* **Fluid vs. Crystallized Intelligence:**
    * *Fluid Intelligence:* The capacity to learn new ways of solving problems and performing activities (declines with age).
    * *Crystallized Intelligence:* Accumulated knowledge of the world acquired throughout our lives (increases with age).
[Image of the structure of intelligence: g factor and specific abilities]

### 9.2 Alternative Theories of Intelligence
Modern psychologists argue that intelligence is more than just raw cognitive speed.
* **Sternberg’s Triarchic Theory:**
    1.  **Analytical:** Problem-solving and logical reasoning.
    2.  **Creative:** Dealing with new situations and generating novel ideas.
    3.  **Practical:** "Street smarts" and managing everyday tasks.
* **Gardner’s Multiple Intelligences:** Eight distinct intelligences, including:
    * *Linguistic, Logical-mathematical, Musical, Spatial, Bodily-kinesthetic, Interpersonal, Intrapersonal, and Naturalistic.*
[Image of Howard Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences]
* **Emotional Intelligence (EQ):** The ability to identify, assess, manage, and control one’s own emotions and the emotions of others.

### 9.3 Measuring Intelligence: IQ Testing
* **IQ (Intelligence Quotient):** A measure of intelligence derived from standardized tests. 
    * *Formula (Original):* (Mental Age / Chronological Age) x 100.
* **Standardization and The Bell Curve:** Most IQ scores fall within a "normal distribution" (Mean = 100, SD = 15).
    * *Intellectual Disability:* IQ below 70 + deficits in adaptive functioning.
    * *Giftedness:* IQ above 130.
* **The Flynn Effect:** The observation that average IQ scores have increased worldwide over the past several decades.

### 9.4 Differences in Intelligence and Stereotype Threat
* **Nature vs. Nurture:** Intelligence is highly heritable (approx. 40-80%), but environment (socioeconomic status, education) plays a massive role in reaching that potential.
* **Stereotype Threat:** Performance decrements that are caused by the knowledge of cultural stereotypes (e.g., if a group is told they are "bad at math," their performance on a math test declines due to anxiety).

### 9.5 Language: The Architecture of Thought
* **Components of Language:**
    * *Phonemes:* The smallest unit of sound (e.g., "b," "th").
    * *Morphemes:* The smallest unit of meaning (e.g., "re-," "ing").
    * *Syntax:* Rules for combining words into sentences.
    * *Context/Pragmatics:* How we derive meaning from social context.
* **The Biology of Language:**
    * *Broca’s Area:* Speech production (Frontal lobe).
    * *Wernicke’s Area:* Language comprehension (Temporal lobe).
* **Theories of Language Acquisition:**
    * *Skinner (Learning):* Language is learned through reinforcement and imitation.
    * *Chomsky (Nativist):* Humans have an innate **Language Acquisition Device (LAD)** and a "Universal Grammar."
* **Critical Period:** There is a sensitive window in early childhood for learning language; after this window closes, language acquisition is significantly harder.

# Chapter 10: Emotions and Motivations
This chapter explores **emotions**—mental states that include physical arousal and cognitive appraisal—and **motivations**, the internal and external forces that direct behavior toward a goal.

### 10.1 The Nature and Theories of Emotion
* **Emotion:** A mental and physiological state that directs our attention and guides our behavior.
* **Basic Emotions:** Fast-acting, evolutionary emotions (e.g., fear, anger, joy, sadness, disgust, surprise) processed via the **limbic system**.
* **Secondary Emotions:** Cognitive emotions that require more processing (e.g., guilt, shame, pride) processed via the **cerebral cortex**.


[Image of the structure of the limbic system in the brain]


#### Theories of Emotion
| Theory | Explanation | Sequence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cannon-Bard** | Arousal and emotion occur simultaneously but independently. | Stimulus -> Arousal + Emotion |
| **James-Lange** | Emotion is our awareness of our physiological responses. | Stimulus -> Arousal -> Emotion |
| **Schachter-Singer (Two-Factor)** | Emotion requires physiological arousal AND a cognitive label. | Stimulus -> Arousal + Cognitive Label -> Emotion |


### 10.2 Stress: The Health Challenge
* **Stress:** The physiological and psychological response to internal or external stressors.
* **General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS):** Selye’s three-stage response to stress:
    1.  **Alarm:** Immediate reaction (Sympathetic nervous system).
    2.  **Resistance:** Adapting to and coping with the stressor.
    3.  **Exhaustion:** Physical resources are depleted; vulnerability to disease increases.
* **HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal):** The body’s primary stress response system involving the release of **Cortisol**.


### 10.3 Positive Psychology: Finding Happiness
* **Happiness:** Determined by genetic set points (approx. 50%), circumstances (10%), and intentional activities (40%).
* **Affective Forecasting:** People are notoriously bad at predicting how future events will make them feel; we overestimate the duration of both positive and negative emotional impacts.
* **Happiness Correlations:** High correlations with social connections, health, and flow; low correlations with wealth (after basic needs are met), physical attractiveness, and age.

### 10.4 Basic Motivations: Eating and Sex
Motivation can be intrinsic or extrinsic and is influenced by biological and social factors.

#### Eating and Hunger
* **Appetite Hormones:**
    * *Insulin/Ghrelin:* Increase hunger.
    * *Leptin/PYY:* Decrease hunger.
* **Eating Disorders:**
    * *Anorexia Nervosa:* Fear of gaining weight leading to starvation.
    * *Bulimia Nervosa:* Binge eating followed by purging.
    * *Binge Eating Disorder:* Distress over binging without purging.

#### Sexual Motivation
* **Sexual Response Cycle (Masters & Johnson):** Excitement, Plateau, Orgasm, and Resolution.
* **Sexual Orientation:** Research suggests a strong biological/genetic component, with environmental factors playing a secondary role. 
* **Kinsey Scale:** Proposes that sexual orientation exists on a continuum rather than as binary categories.

# Chapter 11: Personality
This chapter examines **personality**—an individual’s consistent patterns of feeling, thinking, and behaving—and the various theories that explain its origin and structure.

### 11.1 Personality as Traits
* **Personality Trait:** A relatively stable characteristic that causes individuals to behave in certain ways.
* **The Big Five Factor Model (OCEAN):** The most accurate approximation of basic personality dimensions today.
    1.  **Openness to Experience:** Curiosity, imagination, and interest in variety.
    2.  **Conscientiousness:** Self-discipline, dutifulness, and goal-striving.
    3.  **Extraversion:** Sociability, assertiveness, and excitement-seeking.
    4.  **Agreeableness:** Cooperativeness and trustworthiness.
    5.  **Neuroticism:** Tendency to experience negative emotions (anxiety, anger).
* **Situational Influences:** The *Interactionist Approach* suggests behavior is a result of both stable personality traits and immediate situational variables.

### 11.2 Psychodynamic Theories (Freud and Beyond)
Focuses on the role of the **unconscious mind** and early childhood experiences.
* **The Structure of Mind (Iceberg Model):**
    * *Id:* Primitive impulses (Pleasure principle).
    * *Ego:* Rational part of the mind (Reality principle).
    * *Superego:* Moral compass/conscience (Idealistic principle).
[Image of Freud's iceberg model of the mind]
* **Defense Mechanisms:** Unconscious psychological strategies to reduce anxiety:
    * *Repression:* Pushing arousing thoughts into the unconscious.
    * *Projection:* Attributing one's own unacceptable impulses to others.
    * *Displacement:* Diverting impulses toward a safer target (e.g., yelling at a partner after a bad day at work).
    * *Sublimation:* Channeling impulses into socially acceptable activities (e.g., sports).
* **Psychosexual Stages:** Oral, Anal, Phallic, Latency, and Genital.
* **Neo-Freudians:** Jung (Collective Unconscious), Adler (Inferiority Complex), and Horney (Social Security).

### 11.3 Humanism and Self-Actualization
Humanistic psychology emphasizes free will, personal growth, and the inherent goodness of people.
* **Abraham Maslow:** Proposed the **Hierarchy of Needs**. Self-actualization is the ultimate goal—achieving one's full potential.
    * *Needs Order:* Physiological -> Safety -> Love/Belonging -> Esteem -> Self-Actualization.
[Image of Maslow's hierarchy of needs]
* **Carl Rogers:** Emphasized the **Self-Concept**.
    * *Unconditional Positive Regard:* An attitude of total acceptance toward another person, regardless of their behavior.
    * *Self-Discrepancy Theory:* Emotions arise from the gap between the *Actual Self* and the *Ideal Self* (dejection) or *Ought Self* (agitation).

### 11.4 The Biology of Personality: Genetics
* **Behavioral Genetics:** Studying the effects of genetics on behavior using twin and adoption studies.
    * *Heritability:* Approx. 40-50% of the variance in Big Five personality traits is due to genetics.
    * *Shared vs. Nonshared Environment:* Nonshared environment (unique experiences) has a greater impact on personality than shared family environment.
* **Molecular Genetics:** Identifying specific genes that correlate with personality traits (e.g., genes related to dopamine or serotonin regulation).

# Chapter 12: Defining Psychological Disorders
This chapter explores how psychologists define, diagnose, and categorize **psychological disorders**—patterns of behavioral or psychological symptoms that impact multiple areas of life and create distress for the person experiencing them.

### 12.1 Psychological Disorders: What is Abnormal?
* **Psychological Disorder:** A dynamic process involving an ongoing pattern of thought, emotion, and behavior that is deviant, distressful, dysfunctional, and dangerous.
* **The Bio-Psycho-Social Model:** A perspective that assumes biological, psychological, and social-cultural factors combine and interact to produce psychological disorders.
    * *Biological:* Genes, brain structure, and chemistry.
    * *Psychological:* Stress, trauma, learned helplessness, mood-related perceptions.
    * *Social-Cultural:* Roles, expectations, definitions of normality and disorder.
[Image of the bio-psycho-social model of mental health]

### 12.2 Classifying Disorders: The DSM-5
* **DSM (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders):** The standard manual for classifying and diagnosing psychological disorders. 
* **The Pros and Cons of Labeling:**
    * *Pros:* Provides a common language for clinicians; aids in research and treatment selection.
    * *Cons:* Labels can create bias, stigmatization, and self-fulfilling prophecies.

### 12.3 Anxiety, OCD, and PTSD
* **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD):** Continually tense and apprehensive; autonomic nervous system arousal.
* **Panic Disorder:** Unpredictable, minutes-long episodes of intense dread (panic attacks).
* **Phobias:** Persistent, irrational fear and avoidance of a specific object, activity, or situation.
* **Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD):** Characterized by unwanted repetitive thoughts (**obsessions**) and/or actions (**compulsions**).
* **Posttraumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD):** Haunting memories, nightmares, and social withdrawal that linger for four weeks or more after a traumatic experience.

### 12.4 Mood Disorders
* **Major Depressive Disorder:** A state of hopelessness and lethargy lasting two weeks or more.
* **Bipolar Disorder:** Alternating between the hopelessness and lethargy of depression and the overexcited state of **mania**.
* **Understanding Mood Disorders:**
    * *Biological Perspective:* Focuses on genetic predispositions and neurotransmitters (e.g., Norepinephrine, Serotonin).
    * *Social-Cognitive Perspective:* Explores the "vicious cycle" of depression: Negative stressful events -> Pessimistic explanatory style -> Hopeless state -> Hampered behavior.

### 12.5 Schizophrenia: The Edge of Reality
* **Schizophrenia:** A group of severe disorders characterized by disorganized and delusional thinking, disturbed perceptions, and inappropriate emotions and actions.
* **Symptoms:**
    * *Hallucinations:* Sensory experiences without sensory stimulation (mostly auditory).
    * *Delusions:* False beliefs (e.g., delusions of persecution).
    * *Negative Symptoms:* Absence of appropriate behaviors (e.g., flat affect, social withdrawal).
* **Causes:** Linked to **Dopamine** overactivity, abnormal brain anatomy, and prenatal viral infections.

### 12.6 Personality and Other Disorders
* **Personality Disorders:** Inflexible and enduring behavior patterns that impair social functioning.
    * *Antisocial Personality Disorder:* Lack of conscience for wrongdoing; aggressive or ruthless behavior.
    * *Borderline Personality Disorder:* Instability of interpersonal relationships, self-image, and emotions.
* **Dissociative Disorders:** Conscious awareness becomes separated (dissociated) from previous memories, thoughts, and feelings.
* **Neurodevelopmental Disorders:**
    * *ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder):* Extreme inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity.
    * *Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD):* Significant deficiencies in communication and social interaction.

# Chapter 13: Treating Psychological Disorders
This chapter focuses on the various methods psychologists and medical professionals use to treat psychological disorders, ranging from "talk therapy" to physical brain interventions.

### 13.1 Psychological Therapies (Psychotherapy)
Psychotherapy is the professional treatment for psychological disorders through techniques designed to encourage communication of conflicts and insight.

#### Psychodynamic Therapy
* **Goal:** To help the patient explore the unconscious mind and develop insight into deep-seated conflicts.
* **Key Techniques:**
    * *Free Association:* The patient shares thoughts without censorship.
    * *Dream Analysis:* Exploring the latent content of dreams.
    * *Transference:* The patient's unconscious redirection of feelings from personal relationships toward the therapist.

#### Humanistic Therapy (Client-Centered)
* **Goal:** Emphasizes the importance of the client's current feelings and personal growth.
* **Core Principles (Carl Rogers):**
    * *Unconditional Positive Regard:* Total acceptance of the client.
    * *Empathy:* Seeing the world through the client's eyes.
    * *Genuineness:* Therapist's authenticity in the session.

#### Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
* **Goal:** Focuses on current thoughts (**cognitive**) and behaviors (**behavioral**) rather than childhood history.
* **Techniques:**
    * *Systematic Desensitization:* A behavioral technique used to treat phobias by pairing relaxation with a **Hierarchy of Fears**. 
    * *Exposure Therapy:* Repeatedly presenting the feared stimulus until habituation occurs.
    * *Cognitive Restructuring:* Identifying and challenging irrational thought patterns (e.g., catastrophizing).

### 13.2 Biomedical Therapies
Treatments that focus on reducing symptoms through medication or brain intervention.

#### Drug Therapies (Pharmacotherapy)
| Class | Function | Brand Examples | Used For |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Antidepressants** | Increase Serotonin/Norepinephrine. | Prozac, Zoloft, Lexapro | Depression, Anxiety, OCD. |
| **Antipsychotics** | Block Dopamine receptors. | Thorazine, Haldol, Zyprexa | Schizophrenia. |
| **Mood Stabilizers** | Regulate mood swings. | Lithium, Depakene | Bipolar Disorder. |
| **Anxiolytics** | Increase GABA activity. | Xanax, Valium, Ativan | Panic Disorder, GAD. |

#### Direct Brain Intervention
* **Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT):** A brief electrical current is sent through the brain of an anesthetized patient. Highly effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression.
* **Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS):** Using magnetic pulses to stimulate specific brain regions. Non-invasive and used for depression.

### 13.3 Group, Couples, and Family Therapy
* **Group Therapy:** Clients with similar issues meet with a therapist. Provides social support and a sense of "universality."
* **Family/Couples Therapy:** Focuses on the system of relationships rather than just the individual.

### 13.4 Outcome Research: Does Therapy Work?
* **Meta-Analysis:** Statistical technique that combines results from many different studies.
* **Findings:** Psychotherapy is generally effective, with the average treated person being better off than 75-80% of untreated individuals. 
* **The "Dodo Bird" Verdict:** While specific therapies are better for certain issues (e.g., CBT for phobias), many therapies are equally effective because they share **Common Factors** (e.g., the therapeutic alliance, hope, and a new perspective).

# Chapter 14: Psychology in Our Social Lives
This chapter explores **social psychology**—the scientific study of how we think about, influence, and relate to one another. It covers everything from first impressions to group-level phenomena like obedience and altruism.

### 14.1 Social Thinking and Perceiving Others
* **Social Cognition:** How we interpret, remember, and use information about the social world.
* **Causal Attribution:** The process of trying to determine the causes of people's behavior.
    * *Fundamental Attribution Error:* The tendency to overestimate the role of person factors and underestimate the role of situations when judging others.
* **Attitudes:** Our relatively enduring evaluations of people and objects.
    * *Cognitive Dissonance:* Discomfort felt when we hold two or more conflicting cognitions (e.g., behaviors and beliefs). To reduce it, we often change our attitudes to match our actions.

### 14.2 Social Influence: Conformity and Obedience
* **Conformity:** A change in belief or behavior as a result of real or imagined group pressure.
    * *Asch's Line Studies:* Demonstrated that people will often conform to a group's wrong answer even when the correct answer is obvious.
* **Obedience:** Following direct commands from an authority figure.
    * *Milgram’s Experiments:* Showed that a high percentage of people would administer seemingly lethal electric shocks to a stranger simply because an authority figure told them to do so.
* **Factors Increasing Influence:** Unanimity of the group, high status of the authority figure, and the physical presence of the influencer.

### 14.3 Helping and Aggression
* **Altruism:** Behavior that is intended to help others, especially without any expectation of personal gain.
    * *The Bystander Effect:* The finding that a person is less likely to provide help when there are other bystanders present.
    * *Diffusion of Responsibility:* No one person takes action because everyone assumes someone else will.

* **Aggression:** Behavior intended to harm another individual.
    * *Biological Factors:* Linked to the Amygdala and Testosterone levels.
    * *Environmental Factors:* Negative experiences (frustration, heat, pain) and viewing violent media increase aggressive tendencies.

### 14.4 Working Together in Groups
* **Social Facilitation:** The tendency to perform tasks better or faster in the presence of others (usually for well-learned tasks).
* **Social Inhibition:** Performing worse in the presence of others (usually for complex or new tasks).
* **Social Loafing:** A group process loss that occurs when people do not work as hard in a group as they do when working alone.
* **Groupthink:** A phenomenon that occurs when a group makes faulty decisions because group pressures lead to a deterioration of "mental efficiency, reality testing, and moral judgment."


### 14.5 Close Relationships: Liking and Loving
* **Interpersonal Attraction:** What makes us like others?
    * *Proximity:* We like people who are physically near us.
    * *Similarity:* "Birds of a feather flock together" is more accurate than "opposites attract."
    * *Physical Attractiveness:* We tend to attribute positive qualities (intelligence, kindness) to attractive people (the "What is Beautiful is Good" stereotype).
* **Mere Exposure Effect:** The tendency to prefer stimuli (including people) that we have seen more frequently.
